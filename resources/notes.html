Why Note-Taking Still Matters in the Age of AI

~5 min read Series: Wall of Thoughts

In an age where AI can summarize, explain, and even write, the humble act of taking notes can feel old-fashioned. But note-taking is not about recording information—it’s about processing it. The students who learn to think on paper are the ones who thrive when the worksheet disappears and the real work begins.

The Worksheet Trap: Mistaking Compliance for Comprehension

Many students today mistake access for understanding. If an AI can produce a solution instantly, the learning feels complete. But learning is not the ability to retrieve information—it’s the capacity to reconstruct it when needed. That reconstruction only happens when we slow down enough to think, connect, and explain to ourselves what’s going on.

When I provide professional development workshops for high-school teachers, my first request is simple: please limit the usage of worksheets. They are efficient for grading but often flatten thinking into mere compliance. Worksheets train students to complete, not to comprehend.

A ‘Rude Shock’: The High-School to College Gap

I have the privilege of mentoring high-school students and, at the same time, teaching college-level math—especially the first two years. I see the transition up close, and every semester, I see the same story: bright students get a rude shock in their first university term.

They are worksheet-ready. They are trained to find the right answer in a fill-in-the-blank format. But when faced with a 50‑minute lecture, a dense proof, or the plethora of information thrown at them, they struggle to keep up. The 'worksheet' is gone. In college, there are few (if any) worksheets; they are solely used as supplemental learning materials, not the backbone of instruction. The students who succeed are not necessarily those with the highest marks; they are those who can actively listen, synthesize ideas, and connect them in their own notes.

Yes, I’m Tech-Savvy. And Still a Pen-and-Paper Guy.

This might sound old-fashioned, but it comes from someone who is deeply embedded in technology. As someone who develops games, visualizations, animations, and various learning artefacts, I use AI and technology far more than a regular educator would. Yet, I am firmly a pen-and-paper guy for two critical tasks:

  • Learning something new and mastering it. Writing clarifies thought, exposes gaps, and strengthens memory.
  • Scheduling and journaling my hectic life. Appointments, to-dos, and deadlines—keeping them on paper keeps me honest and focused.

My Approach: The Best of Two Worlds

I often go on like a broken record with my high-school students about the need for taking notes. Many don’t see the fruits of it until later, but this philosophy comes directly from my own journey. After 20 years of education in India and my research in STEM Education in the U.S., I always aim for a productive mix of both worlds. This is the core of our philosophy at ActiveSTEM:

  • From India: The discipline of well-structured, organized notes that become a personal knowledge dump you can revisit anytime; and the early-stage rigor in mathematics that builds an unshakeable foundation.
  • From the U.S.: The creativity and application-heavy, real-world mathematics that asks students to model, argue, and connect ideas across contexts.

This blend is where students truly soar: disciplined note-craft plus authentic, challenging problems yields durable understanding. This is the training that bridges the gap to college.

Why I Wrote Extreme Algebra

Over time, I realized that many of the gaps I observe—especially in algebraic fluency and mathematical writing—begin much earlier than college. To address this, I wrote Extreme Algebra: 200 Non-Routine Problems Inspired by Global Math Traditions, a book for motivated students in Grades 6–8. It’s drawn from the rigorous traditions of India’s NCERT, Singapore’s structured practice, and Europe’s GCSE systems.

The goal is simple: rebuild confidence in symbolic manipulation, pattern recognition, and clear mathematical writing. These are precisely the skills that crumble when students grow up on fill-in-the-blank worksheets. The book encourages students to write, reason, and revise—building the kind of fluency that lasts beyond exams.

In many ways, Extreme Algebra is my response to what I see daily in classrooms: capable students who understand concepts but hesitate when they must write them down. A well-kept notebook bridges that gap—just as much as any modern AI tool can accelerate understanding, pen-and-paper reasoning cements it.

Beyond Worksheets: Smart Notes in Practice

Call it “smart notes” if you like: capture ideas in your own language, connect them to prior knowledge, and keep entries short, clear, and linkable. Over time, this becomes a web of understanding you can draw on when tackling new problems. AI can accelerate retrieval; these notes cultivate reasoning.

In an age of instant answers, my real job as an educator is not to teach information, but to teach thinking. That is the skill that lasts a lifetime.

For Parents: How to Help Your Child Start Taking Notes

In my experience, most students don’t resist note-taking because they are lazy—they resist it because no one has shown them what good notes can do. Parents can play a quiet but powerful role in building this habit, especially in the middle and high school years when structure and ownership of learning start to take shape.

  • Start small and visible: Get one good notebook for each subject—not a stack of loose papers. Ask your child to maintain a “math log” or “science wall” where they write down one reflection a day after class: what confused them, what clicked, and what formula or idea felt powerful.
  • Make it personal, not perfect: Notes should not look like a textbook. Let them doodle, color-code, or summarize in their own language. When they explain an idea in their own words, they begin to own it.
  • Use class material, not generic prompts: Instead of giving “note-taking strategies,” ask: “Show me how your teacher explained this problem.” It shifts focus from appearance to comprehension.
  • Encourage revision, not rewriting: Every few days, let your child revisit their notes and mark questions or confusions. The goal is to build curiosity, not a decorative notebook.
  • Model the behavior: If you work or plan with pen and paper, let them see you do it. When parents take notes, children realize it’s a lifelong tool, not a school task.

Over time, these small routines grow into a discipline. Students start finding joy in clarity—and the act of thinking on paper becomes their quiet advantage.
