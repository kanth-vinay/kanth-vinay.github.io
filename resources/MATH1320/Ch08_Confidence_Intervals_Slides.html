    Estimating a Population Mean ($\mu$)

    Estimating the Unknown

    Point Estimate:
    A single value of a statistic used to estimate a parameter.
    (e.g., Using $\bar{x}$ to estimate $\mu$)
    Confidence Interval (CI):
    An interval of numbers obtained from a point estimate, used to estimate the parameter.
    • Confidence Level: The confidence we have that the parameter lies in the interval.
    • CI Estimate: The confidence level AND the confidence interval.

    I. C.I. when $\sigma$ Is Known

    The Foundation (from Normal Distributions)

    Remember the normal distribution logic regarding sample means ($\bar{x}$):

    Approximately $95\%$ of all samples have means within $2$ standard deviations of $\mu$.

    More precisely, $100(1-\alpha)\%$ of all samples have means within $z_{\alpha/2}$standard deviations of $\mu$.

    The One-Mean $z$-Interval Procedure

    Purpose & Assumptions

    Purpose: To find a CI for a population mean, $\mu$.

    Assumptions:

    1. Simple random sample
    2. Normal population OR large sample ($n \ge 30$)
    3. $\sigma$ is KNOWN.

    The 3 Steps

    1Find Critical Value: For confidence level $1-\alpha$, use the normal table to find $z_{\alpha/2}$.

    2Find the Interval: The CI for $\mu$is:

    $\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

    3Interpret: We are $(1-\alpha)\%$confident that the true population mean $\mu$ falls in this interval.

    When to Use the $z$-Interval

    Sample Size ($n$)Guideline for Use
    Small ($n <15$)Use ONLY when the variable is normally distributed (or very close).
    Moderate ($15 \le n \le 30$)Use UNLESS the data contain outliers or the variable is far from normal.
    Large ($n \ge 30$)Use essentially without restriction. (Watch out for severe outliers).
    Fundamental Principle of Data Analysis:
    Before performing statistical inference, EXAMINE the sample data(e.g., plot it). If conditions are violated, DO NOT apply the procedure. Use a more appropriate one.

    Margin of Error ($E$)

    Definition

    The margin of error for the estimate of $\mu$ is $E$. It is the "plus or minus"part of the interval.

    $E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

    Interval is computed as:$\bar{x}\pm E$

    Trade-offs & Accuracy

    • Critical Value: The critical value ($z$ or $t$) increaseswith higher confidence levels.
    • Margin of Error: A larger critical value results in alarger margin of error ($E$).
    • Wider Interval: The margin of error, added to and subtracted from the point estimate, creates the confidence interval. Therefore, a higherconfidence level leads to a wider interval.
    • Confidence Level (Trade-off):
      fixed sample size $\rightarrow$ decreased confidence level $\rightarrow$ decreased$E$
      (Improves interval tightness, but less confident).
    • Sample Size($n$):
      fixed confidence level $\rightarrow$ increased sample size $\rightarrow$ decreased$E$
      (Improves the accuracy of the estimate through more data).

    Determining Required Sample Size ($n$)

    If we want a specific Margin of Error ($E$) at a specific Confidence Level($1-\alpha$), we can solve for $n$:

    $n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{E}\right)^2$

    Crucial Rule

    ALWAYS ROUND UP!

    If your calculation gives $n = 41.12$, you must take $n = 42$ to ensure your margin of error requirement is strictly met.

    II. C.I. when $\sigma$ is UNKNOWN

    The Reality of Statistics

    In real life, if we don't know the population mean ($\mu$), we almost certainly don't know the population standard deviation ($\sigma$).

    The Solution: Student's $t$-Distribution

    We replace $\sigma$ with our sample standard deviation $s$. The standard normal$z$ is replaced with the $t$-statistic:

    $t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{s/\sqrt{n}}$

    Degrees of Freedom (df) = $n -1$

    Properties of $t$-Curves

    • Area: Total area under curve = $1$.
    • Asymptotes: Extends indefinitely, never touches x-axis.
    • Symmetry: Symmetric about $0$.
    • Convergence: As df ($n-1$) gets larger, the $t$-curve looks increasingly like the standard normal $z$-curve.
    The t-dist has "fatter tails" than Normal for small n.

    The One-Mean $t$-Interval Procedure

    Assumptions

    1. Simple random sample
    2. Normal pop. OR large sample ($n \ge 30$)
    3. $\sigma$ is UNKNOWN.

    The 3 Steps

    1Critical Value: Find$t_{\alpha/2}$with $df = n-1$.

    2Interval: Compute CI:

    $\bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$

    3Interpret.

    NOTE: Exact for normal populations; approx. correct for large samples from nonnormal pops.

    The Role of Degrees of Freedom ($df$)

    Degrees of Freedom ($df = n-1$) determines the shape of the $t$-distribution.
    Smaller $df$ means we are less certain, resulting in "fatter tails". We compensate by using wider intervals.


    Standard Normal ($z$)
    Area in tails (beyond $\pm 1.96$): 5.00%
    $t$-Curve (df=4)
    Area in tails (beyond $\pm 1.96$): 12.16%

    Key Takeaways

    • Range: A confidence interval provides a range in which the population mean is likely to fall.
    • CI Estimate: Includes the confidence level AND the confidence interval.
    • Critical Value: The critical value($z$ or $t$) increases with higher confidence levels.
    • Margin of Error: A larger critical value results in a larger margin of error ($E$).
    • Interval Width: A higher confidence level leads to a wider interval.
    • Sample Size Impact:Increasing sample size reduces confidence interval width, making estimates more precise!

    R Code: One-Mean $z$-Interval

    Manual calculation assuming $\sigma$ is known.

    # Data & Parameters
data <- c(54, 57, 53, 59, 58)
sigma <- 5            # Population Std Dev is KNOWN
alpha <- 0.05         # For 95% Confidence Level

n <- length(data)
x_bar <- mean(data)

# 1. Critical Value: 
# Using qnorm for the normal distribution
z_star <- qnorm(1 - alpha/2)

# 2. Margin of Error
ME <- z_star * (sigma / sqrt(n))

# 3. Report the Interval
z_star
ME
c(x_bar - ME, x_bar + ME)

    R Code: One-Mean $t$-Interval

    Manual calculation assuming $\sigma$ is UNKNOWN (we use $s$).

    # Data & Parameters
data <- c(54, 57, 53, 59, 58)
alpha <- 0.05         # For 95% Confidence Level

n <- length(data)
x_bar <- mean(data)
s <- sd(data)         # We calculate Sample Std Dev from data
df <- n - 1           # Degrees of Freedom

# 1. Critical Value: 
# Using qt for the t-distribution
t_star <- qt(1 - alpha/2, df)

# 2. Margin of Error
ME <- t_star * (s / sqrt(n))

# 3. Report the Interval
t_star
ME
c(x_bar - ME, x_bar + ME)
