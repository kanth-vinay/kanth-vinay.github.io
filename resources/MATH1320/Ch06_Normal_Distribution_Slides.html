    I. Introduction to Density Curves

    Key Concept

    A smooth curve that describes the overall pattern of a distribution.

    2 Critical Properties

    1. Always Positive: Curve is on or above the x-axis.
    2. Total Area = 1: The area under the curve is exactly 1 (100%).
    Area = Probability = Percentage
    The area under the curve for a range represents the % of observations in that range.

    Visual: Area = %

    Shaded region is the proportion of data between $a$ and $b$.

    II. The Normal Distribution

    Definition

    A variable is Normally Distributed if its distribution has the specific bell-shape of a normal curve.

    Defined by Mean ($\mu$) and Standard Deviation ($\sigma$).

    Real-World Example: Heights

    Frequencies are low at extremes ($<$57" or $>$73") and peak in the middle(63"-65").

    Histogram vs. Curve

    The smooth curve fits the shape of the data bars.

    III. Standard Normal Distribution

    The "Standard" ($Z$)

    A special normal distribution with parameters:

    Mean ($\mu$) = 0
    St. Dev ($\sigma$) = 1

    Standardizing Formula (Z-Score)

    $z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$

    This maps ANY normal curve onto the single Standard Normal Curve.

    Mathematical Form (Early 19th Century)

    $$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

    Constant $\sqrt{2\pi}$ from integrating exponential function.

    Visual Standardization

    Standard Normal: Centered at 0, Ticks at $\pm 1, 2, 3$.

    IV. Properties & Z-Table

    Key Properties

    • Total Area = 1 (100%): Represents the probability of all possible observations.
    • Asymptotes: Extends forever, never touches axis.
    • Symmetry: Mirror image around 0.
    • Concentration: Almost all area between -3 and 3.

    Using Table II

    Table gives area to the LEFT.

    • Left Area: Direct from table.
    • Right Area: $1 - \text{TableValue}$.
    • Between: $\text{Area}(z_2) - \text{Area}(z_1)$.

    $z_\alpha$ Notation

    $z_{0.05}$ means Area to RIGHT is 0.05

    Example: $z_{0.025} = 1.96$

    Example: $z_{0.05} = 1.645$

    V. Finding Probabilities

    The Procedure

    1. Sketch the curve, label mean.
    2. Shade region of interest.
    3. Find Z-Scores using $z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$.
    4. Use Table II to find area.

    Example: IQ Scores

    $\mu=100, \sigma=15$. Find % between 115 and 140.

    $z_1 = \frac{115-100}{15} = 1.00$
    $z_2 = \frac{140-100}{15} = 2.67$

    R Commands: pnorm vs qnorm

    pnorm(q, mean, sd)

    Input: Value ($x$) → Output: Area (Prob)

    Input: Area (Prob) → Output: Value ($x$)

    Inverse: Finding Observations ($x$)

    The Procedure

    1. Sketch curve & Shade area.
    2. Use Table II to find $z$-score matching the area.
    3. Convert back using:
    $x = \mu + z \cdot \sigma$

    Example

    Find score for top 10% ($\mu=50, \sigma=5$).

    1. Top 10% = Bottom 90% (Area = 0.90).

    2. Look up 0.90 in table $\to z \approx 1.28$.

    3. Calculate:

    $x = 50 + 1.28(5) = 56.4$

    VII. Normal Approx. to Binomial

    The Concept

    Binomial (Discrete) looks like Normal (Continuous) when $n$ is large.

    Conditions

    $np \ge 5$
    AND
    $nq \ge 5$

    Parameters

    $\mu = np$

    $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$

    Continuity Correction

    Adjust discrete bar to continuous area.

    $P(X=7) \approx P(6.5 < X < 7.5)$

    VII. Sampling Error

    Definitions

    Sampling Error:
    Error resulting from using a sample to estimate a population characteristic.
    Sampling Distribution of Sample Mean:
    For a variable x and sample size n, the distribution of the variable $\bar{x}$ is called the sampling distribution of the sample mean.

    Key Fact: Sample Size

    The larger the sample size ($n$), the smaller the sampling error tends to be when estimating $\mu$ by $\bar{x}$.

    Notation

    • $\mu$: Population Mean
    • $\bar{x}$: Sample Mean
    • $n$: Sample Size

    VIII. Mean & Std Dev of $\bar{x}$

    Mean of Sample Mean

    $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$

    The mean of the variable $\bar{x}$ equals the mean of the variable under consideration.

    Std Dev of Sample Mean

    $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

    The standard deviation of $\bar{x}$ equals the population standard deviation divided by $\sqrt{n}$.

    Also called Standard Error.

    IX. Sampling Dist. Characteristics

    Case 1: Normal Population

    If $x$ is Normally Distributed ($N(\mu, \sigma)$):

    Then for ANY sample size $n$, $\bar{x}$ is normally distributed.

    Mean: $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$

    St.Dev: $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

    Case 2: Any Population (CLT)

    Central Limit Theorem (CLT):

    For a relatively large sample size ($n \ge 30$), the variable $\bar{x}$ isApproximately Normally Distributed regardless of the distribution of variable $x$.

    Approximation improves as $n$ increases.

    Summary: Sampling Distributions

    • Mean: $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$
    • Standard Deviation: $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$
    • Shape:
      • If $x$ is Normal ($N$) $\rightarrow$ $\bar{x}$ is Normal ($N$).
      • If $n$ is Large ($n \ge 30$) $\rightarrow$ $\bar{x}$ is Approx. Normal ($N$).
