    12.1 One Population Proportion

    Consider a population where each member either has or does not have a specified attribute (e.g., "voted", "defective").

    Definition 12.1

    • Population proportion, $p$: The true proportion of the entire population that has the specified attribute.
    • Sample proportion, $\hat{p}$: The proportion of a sample from the population that has the specified attribute.

    Formula 12.1: $\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}$

    $x = \text{successes}$, $n= \text{sample size}$

    Visualizing a Proportion

    Change the number of "successes" ($x$) out of$n=25$.

    Why strictly the $z$-table? (Means vs. Proportions)

    Means ($\mu, \bar{x}$)

    • Data: Quantitative (e.g., height, temperature)
    • Std. Error: Uses $s/\sqrt{n}$
    • Distribution: Requires the $t$-distribution because the sample standard deviation ($s$) must be estimated from data independently of the mean.

    Proportions ($p, \hat{p}$)

    • Data: Categorical (Success/Failure, Yes/No)
    • Std. Error: Uses $\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}$
    • Distribution:Always$z$-distribution! The variance is perfectly locked to the proportion itself, so there is no independent $s$ to estimate.
    Golden Rule for Chapter 12: We never use the $t$-table! Proportions always use the$z$-table.
    Just remember your checks: $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$.

    Procedure 12.1: One-Proportion z-Interval

    Assumptions

    • Simple random sample
    • The number of successes, $x$, and failures, $n-x$, are both 5 or greater.

    Margin of Error (Formula 12.2)

    $$E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$$

    The Procedure

    1. Find $z_{\alpha/2}$ for confidence level $1-\alpha$ using the normal table.
    2. The interval bounds for $p$ are:
      $\hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$
    3. Interpret the interval in context.

    Formula 12.3: Finding the Required Sample Size

    How large a sample do we need to estimate a population proportion within a chosen margin of error $E$?

    Scenario 1: No Guess Available

    If we have no prior idea what $p$ might be, we assume the worst-case scenario ($p = 0.5$) to ensure the sample is large enough:

    $$n = 0.25 \left( \frac{z_{\alpha/2}}{E} \right)^2$$

    Always round UP to the nearest whole number.

    Scenario 2: Educated Guess

    If we have a prior estimate $\hat{p}_g$ (e.g., from a past study):

    $$n = \hat{p}_g (1-\hat{p}_g) \left( \frac{z_{\alpha/2}}{E} \right)^2$$

    Always round UP to the nearest whole number.

    Procedure 12.2: One-Proportion z-Test

    Seamless Continuation from Ch 10: The 5 core steps of Hypothesis Testing (Hypotheses $\rightarrow$ Critical Value $\rightarrow$ Test Statistic$\rightarrow$ P-Value $\rightarrow$ Conclusion) are IDENTICAL to everything we've done! The only difference is the standard error formula we use for the test statistic.

    Setup & Assumptions

    • Simple random sample
    • $n p_0 \geq 5$ and $n(1-p_0) \geq 5$
    • Null Hypothesis: $H_0: p = p_0$
    • Alt Hypothesis: $H_a: p \neq p_0$ or $p < p_0$ or $p> p_0$

    Test Statistic

    Compute the observed test statistic $z_0$:
    (Notice it is just [Observed - Null] / SE!)

    $$z_0 = \frac{\hat{p}-p_{0}}{\sqrt{p_{0}(1-p_{0})/n}}$$

    Use the normal table to find P-value/CV, then conclude.

    Class Activity: Extract & Execute

    The Scenario: A popular streaming service wants to evaluate mobile app usage. The marketing team publishes a bold claim stating that more than 55% of all current subscribers primarily use the mobile app to watch content. To investigate this internally, a data analyst takes a random sample of 850 active subscribers and finds that precisely 510 of them use the mobile app. At the $\alpha = 0.05$ significance level, does the data support the marketing team's claim?

    Step 1: Read carefully. What kind of data is this? Are we dealing with means or averages? No, we are dealing with proportions (percentages)!

    Extraction &Setup

    • Null Proportion($p_0$): $0.55$
      $\rightarrow H_0: p = 0.55 \quad H_a: p >0.55$ (Right-tailed)
    • Sample Size ($n$):$850$
    • Successes ($x$):$510$
      $\rightarrow \hat{p} = 510 / 850 = 0.60$

    Execution & Decision

    $z_0 = \frac{0.60 - 0.55}{\sqrt{0.55(0.45)/850}} \approx 2.93$

    $2.93 > 1.645$ (CV) $\rightarrow$ Reject $H_0$

    There is sufficient evidence to support the claim.

    Key Fact 12.2: Sampling Dist of $(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2)$

    For independent samples we take from each population (sizes $n_1$ and$n_2$):

    • Mean: $\mu_{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2} = p_1 - p_2$
    • Standard Deviation:
      $\sigma_{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2} = \sqrt{\frac{p_1(1-p_1)}{n_1} +\frac{p_2(1-p_2)}{n_2}}$
    • Shape: Normally distributed for large $n_1$ and $n_2$.

    Procedure 12.3: Two-Proportions z-Test

    Hypotheses & Assumptions

    • $H_0: p_1 = p_2$
    • $H_a:$ $p_1 \neq p_2$, or $p_1 < p_2$, or $p_1> p_2$
    • Independent random samples.
    • All observed counts ($x_1$, $n_1-x_1$, $x_2$, $n_2-x_2$) $\geq 5$.

    Pooled Sample Proportion

    Because $H_0$ states $p_1 = p_2$, we pool the samples to estimate the common proportion:

    $\hat{p}_p = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2}$

    Test Statistic:

    $z = \frac{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_p(1-\hat{p}_p)} \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1} +\frac{1}{n_2}}}$

    Procedure 12.4: Two-Proportions z-Interval

    Confidence Interval Endpoints

    To estimate the difference $(p_1 - p_2)$ with confidence level $1-\alpha$:

    $$(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} +\frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}$$
    Notice the difference: For the confidence interval, we do not pool the sample proportions. We use the individual $\hat{p}_1$ and $\hat{p}_2$ in the standard error, because we are no longer assuming $p_1 = p_2$ (which was the assumption under the null hypothesis).

    R Code: Testing Proportions in RStudio

    R handles proportion tests easily using `prop.test()` and estimating confidence intervals at the same time.

    Proportion Tests
    # --- ONE POPULATION PROPORTION ---
# x: number of successes, n: sample size, p: null proportion
# correct = FALSE turns off continuity correction (matches textbook z-test)
prop.test(x = 42, n = 100, p = 0.50, alternative = "greater", conf.level = 0.95, correct = FALSE)

# --- TWO POPULATION PROPORTIONS ---
# Supply successes as a vector: c(x1, x2)
# Supply sample sizes as a vector: c(n1, n2)
successes <- c(45, 30)
trials    <- c(100, 80)

# Two-tailed test comparing p1 and p2
prop.test(x = successes, n = trials, alternative = "two.sided", conf.level = 0.95, correct = FALSE)

    Practice Sprint: Problem 1

    10 Minute Challenge:
    A poll asked whether states should be allowed to conduct random drug tests on elected officials. Of 16,195 respondents, 65% said "yes".
    a. Determine the margin of error for a 99% confidence interval.
    b. Without doing any calculations, indicate whether the margin of error is larger or smaller for a 90% confidence interval. Explain your answer.

    Solution

    Part (a):
    $n = 16,195 \quad \hat{p} = 0.65$
    For 99% confidence, $z_{\alpha/2} = 2.576$
    $E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = 2.576 \cdot\sqrt{\frac{0.65(0.35)}{16195}} \approx \mathbf{0.0097}$ (or $0.97\%$)

    Part (b):
    The margin of error for a 90% confidence interval would be smaller. To have a lower level of overall confidence, you do not need as wide of an interval to capture the true population proportion. (Mathematically, $z_{\alpha/2}$ for 90% is only $1.645$, which shrinks the margin compared to multiplying by $2.576$).

    Practice Sprint: Problem 2

    10 Minute Challenge:
    For a margin of error of 0.04, a 95% confidence level, and an educated guess of 0.4 for the sample proportion,obtain a sample size that will ensure a margin of error of at most the one specified.

    Solution

    $E = 0.04 \quad \hat{p}_g = 0.4 \quad z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96$ (for 95%)
    $n = \hat{p}_g(1-\hat{p}_g) \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2}}{E}\right)^2 = 0.4(0.6)\left(\frac{1.96}{0.04}\right)^2$
    $n = 0.24 \cdot (49)^2 = 0.24 \cdot 2401 = 576.24$
    Always round up! $n = \mathbf{577}$

    Practice Sprint: Problem 3

    10 Minute Challenge:
    Perform a hypothesis test for $x=35$, $n=50$, $H_0: p=0.6$, $H_a: p < 0.6$, and $\alpha=0.05$.
    a. Determine the sample proportion.
    b. Decide if the one-proportion z-test is appropriate.
    c. If appropriate, determine the test statistic, critical value(s), and conclusion.

    Solution

    Part (a): $\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{35}{50} = \mathbf{0.70}$

    Part (b): We must verify the assumptions ($np_0 \ge 5$ and $n(1-p_0) \ge 5$).
    $50(0.6) = 30 \ge 5$ and $50(0.4) = 20 \ge 5$. It is **appropriate**.

    Part (c): The test statistic is calculated as $z = \frac{0.70 -0.60}{\sqrt{0.60 \cdot 0.40 / 50}} = \mathbf{1.44}$.
    Wait! Look carefully: The alternative hypothesis claimed $p < 0.6$. The sample proportion$0.70$ directly contradicts the claim immediately. The left-tailed P-value is very large($0.925$).
    $0.925 \not\leq 0.05 \rightarrow \mathbf{\text{Do Not Reject } H_0}$.

    For independent samples $x_1=21$, $n_1=40$ and $x_2=25$, $n_2=30$ with $\alpha=0.10$:

    Practice Sprint: Problem 5

    10 Minute Challenge:
    Using the identical dataset mapping to the previous question ($x_1=21$, $n_1=40$, $x_2=25$, $n_2=30$,$\alpha=0.10$):
    c. Conduct a left-tailed hypothesis test (find the test statistic, critical value, and conclusion).
    d. Find an 80% confidence interval for $p_1 - p_2$.

    Solution

    Part (c):
    $z_0 = \frac{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_p(1-\hat{p}_p)}\sqrt{1/n_1 + 1/n_2}} =\frac{0.525 - 0.833}{\sqrt{0.657(0.343)}\sqrt{\frac{1}{40} + \frac{1}{30}}} \approx\mathbf{-2.69}$
    Critical Value for $\alpha=0.10$ left-tailed is $z = \mathbf{-1.28}$.
    $-2.69 \le -1.28$, so we $\mathbf{Reject H_0}$.

    Part (d):
    $CI = (\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}$
    Wait, $z_{\alpha/2}$ for 80% CI is $1.28$.
    $E = 1.28 \cdot \sqrt{\frac{0.525(0.475)}{40} + \frac{0.833(0.167)}{30}} = 1.28 \cdot\sqrt{0.00623 + 0.00463} = 1.28 \cdot 0.1042 = \mathbf{0.133}$
    $CI: -0.308 \pm 0.133 \rightarrow (\mathbf{-0.441, -0.175})$
