    Problem 1 — One-Mean z-Test

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes

    Full Workflow + Assumptions

    A labor economist is studying quarterly net job gain percentages in a specific sector. Over the past 20 quarters, the following sample statistics were computed from the collected data:

    • Sample size: $n = 20$
    • Sample mean net job gain: $\bar{x} = 0.34\%$
    • Population standard deviation (historically established): $\sigma = 0.5\%$

    A commonly cited benchmark suggests that a “healthy” labor market should have an average quarterly job gain of $0.2\%$.

    The economist wants to determine whether the current data provide sufficient evidence that the true mean job gain is greater than $0.2\%$.

    Your Tasks:
    1. Clearly state the null and alternative hypotheses.
    2. Verify whether conditions for using a z-test are met.
    3. Compute the test statistic using R.
    4. Compute the p-value.
    5. Make a decision at significance level $\alpha = 0.05$.
    6. Interpret your result in context of job growth.

    Problem 1 — Answer Key

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes
    • $H_0: \mu = 0.2\%$
    • $H_a: \mu > 0.2\%$ (Right-Tailed)
    R Code
    sigma <- 0.5
mu_0 <- 0.2
x_bar <- 0.34
n <- 20

z <- (x_bar - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
p_right <- 1 - pnorm(z)

z
p_right
    Output & Decision
    > z
[1] 1.252198
> p_right
[1] 0.1052507
    • Decision: Since $p\text{-value} = 0.1053 > (\alpha = 0.05)$, we Do not reject $H_0$.
    • Interpretation: There is not sufficient evidence at the 5% significance level to conclude that the true mean job gain strictly exceeds $0.2\%$.

    Problem 2 — One-Mean z-Test

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes

    Two-Tailed, Structured

    A manufacturing company claims that the average lifetime of its batteries is $29$ hours.

    A consumer group tests a random sample of batteries and records:

    • Sample size: $n = 29$
    • Sample mean lifetime: $\bar{x} = 31$ hours
    • Known population standard deviation: $\sigma = 8$ hours

    The group wants to determine whether the true mean lifetime differs from the company’s claim.

    Your Tasks:
    1. State the null and alternative hypotheses.
    2. Compute the test statistic using R.
    3. Compute the p-value (two-tailed).
    4. Make a decision at $\alpha = 0.05$.
    5. Interpret the result in context of battery performance.

    Problem 2 — Answer Key

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes
    R Code
    sigma <- 8
mu_0 <- 29
x_bar <- 31
n <- 29

z <- (x_bar - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
p_two <- 2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(z)))

z
p_two
    Output & Decision
    > z
[1] 1.346291
> p_two
[1] 0.1782089
    • Decision: Since $p\text{-value} = 0.1782 > (\alpha = 0.05)$, we Do not reject $H_0$.
    • Interpretation: There is not sufficient evidence to conclude that the true mean battery lifetime differs significantly from the company's claim of 29 hours.

    Problem 3 — z-Test Using Critical Values

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes

    A quality control engineer is monitoring a production process where the target mean measurement is $38$ units.

    A sample from the process yields:

    • Sample size: $n = 23$
    • Sample mean: $\bar{x} = 36$
    • Population standard deviation: $\sigma = 8$

    The engineer wants to determine whether the process mean is different from the target.

    Your Tasks:
    1. State hypotheses.
    2. Compute the test statistic.
    3. Determine the critical values for a two-tailed test at $\alpha = 0.05$.
    4. Use the rejection region approach to make a decision.
    5. Interpret the result in context of process control.

    Problem 3 — Answer Key

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes
    R Code (Critical Values)
    sigma <- 8
mu_0 <- 38
x_bar <- 36
n <- 23
alpha <- 0.05

z <- (x_bar - mu_0) / (sigma / sqrt(n))
cv_lower <- qnorm(alpha/2)
cv_upper <- qnorm(1 - alpha/2)

z
cv_lower
cv_upper
    Output & Decision
    > z
[1] -1.198958
> cv_lower
[1] -1.959964
> cv_upper
[1] 1.959964
    • Rejection Region: $z < -1.96$ or $z > 1.96$.
    • Decision: Our $z = -1.199$ is inside the non-rejection region. Do not reject $H_0$.
    • Interpretation: There is not sufficient evidence to conclude the process mean significantly differs from the 38 unit target.

    Problem 4 — Sampling Distribution

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes

    Interpretation and Variability

    A national survey reports that the average annual salary in a certain profession is:

    • Population mean: $\mu = 51.2$ (in thousands)
    • Population standard deviation: $\sigma = 8.8$ (in thousands)

    Researchers are interested in understanding how sample size affects the variability of the sample mean.

    Your Tasks:
    1. For a sample size of $n = 64$: Find the mean of the sampling distribution and the standard deviation (standard error).
    2. For a sample size of $n = 256$: Find the mean of the sampling distribution and the standard deviation.
    3. Compare the variability of the two sampling distributions.
    4. Explain in plain English: What happens to variability as sample size increases? Why this matters in statistical inference?

    Problem 4 — Answer Key

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes
    R Code (Standard Errors)
    sigma <- 8.8

# Standard Error for n = 64
SE_64 <- sigma / sqrt(64)
SE_64

# Standard Error for n = 256
SE_256 <- sigma / sqrt(256)
SE_256
    Outputs
    > SE_64
[1] 1.1

> SE_256
[1] 0.55
    Interpretation:
    • Larger sample $\rightarrow$ smaller variability.
    • Estimates become more stable and precise representations of the true population.

    Problem 5 — Pooled Two-Mean

    Time Limit: 10 Minutes

    Provided below are summary statistics for independent simple random samples from two populations. Use the pooled t-test and the pooled t-interval procedure to conduct the required hypothesis test and obtain the specified confidence interval.

    $$ \bar{x}_1=10,\; s_1=2.4,\; n_1=12 \qquad \bar{x}_2=13,\; s_2=2.3,\; n_2=12 $$
    Your Tasks:
    1. Identify hypotheses for a two-tailed test, $\alpha=0.05$.
    2. Compute the test statistic $t$.
    3. Determine the critical values.
    4. What is the conclusion of the hypothesis test?
    5. Find the 95% confidence interval.

    Problem 5 — Answer Key

    Calculations
    • Hypotheses: $H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2, \; H_a: \mu_1 \ne \mu_2$
    • Test Statistic: $t = -3.126$
    • Critical Values: $\pm 2.074$
    Decisions & CI
    • Decision: Since the test statistic (-3.126) is in the rejection region ($< -2.074$), Reject $H_0$.
    • 95% Confidence Interval: From $-4.990$ to $-1.010$.

    Problem 6 — Answer Key
