Rule of thumb to give Samanvi: "If the answer can only be one of a few things and you're asking 'how much' or 'how often,' use a bar chart. Use a pie chart only when you want to show that several pieces make up a whole, like gender or respondent type."
For the ASA report: ASA judges specifically evaluate whether displays are "appropriate." Pie charts for ordinal frequency data (Never/Rarely/Sometimes) are not appropriate. Selecting the right chart type directly affects the Data Display score.

2025 Winning DNA — Applied to Samanvi's Project

Adapted from ASA 2025 winners · Appropriate for grade 7–9

The winning projects used advanced methods (z-score normalization, ANOVA, spectrophotometry) that are not appropriate for 7th grade. But the underlying principles are fully achievable. Here is how each principle translates.

Show, don't tell

Winners documented the exact mechanics of their data collection — sticky notes, numbered packages, random generators.

For Samanvi: Write one paragraph describing exactly how the survey was shared — which groups, which platforms, who shared it. Don't just say "survey was distributed."

Prove the math

Winners ran tests only after explicitly proving conditions were met.

For Samanvi: Before drawing conclusions, write explicitly: "This is a convenience sample, not a random sample, so I cannot generalize to all children. My findings apply to this specific group."

Control the environment

Winners engineered physical solutions to block confounding variables.

For Samanvi: Acknowledge confounders — parental influence on younger children's answers, response bias (health-conscious families more likely to respond), and the anonymous self-report format.

Embrace flaws

Winners openly analyzed what went wrong and proposed concrete mechanical improvements.

For Samanvi: In the reflection, identify at least two specific improvements — for example, adding a question about screen content type, or targeting a more balanced age distribution.

Practical significance

Winners asked not just "is there a difference?" but "does the difference actually matter?"

For Samanvi: After any percentage comparison, ask and write: "Is this difference large enough to be meaningful? What would this mean for a real family?"

Transparency of sampling

Winners left "zero ambiguity" about how their sample was acquired.

For Samanvi: Include a short paragraph: "I collected responses through WhatsApp community groups and personal outreach. The survey was open for [X] days. Here is the distribution of who responded." Include the raw data table in an appendix.
