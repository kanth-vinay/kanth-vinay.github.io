Problem 1: Bird Arrival Rates

AP Exam Question — As Written

Male birds of a certain species arrive at a nesting area over a thirty-day period. The rate at which the male birds arrive at the nesting area at time $t$ days is modeled by a differentiable function $M$, where $M(t)$ is measured in number of birds per day. Selected values of $M(t)$ are shown in the table.

$t$ (days) 0 5 10 15 20 25 30
$M(t)$ (birds per day) 2 7 16 6 5 2 0

(Note: Your calculator should be in radian mode.)

Part A
Approximate $M'(7.5)$ using the average rate of change of $M$ over the interval $5 \le t \le 10$. Show the work that leads to your answer, and indicate units of measure.

Part B
i. Use a midpoint Riemann sum with the three subintervals $[0, 10]$, $[10, 20]$, and $[20, 30]$ to approximate $\displaystyle\int_0^{30} M(t)\,dt$. Show the work that leads to your answer.
ii. Interpret the meaning of $\displaystyle\int_0^{30} M(t)\,dt$ in the context of the problem.

Part C
The rate at which female birds of the same species arrive at the same nesting area, in birds per day, is modeled by the function $F$ defined as follows.

$$F(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{for } 0 \le t < 15 \\ 18 + 16\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{20}(t + 15)\right) & \text{for } 15 \le t \le 45 \end{cases}$$

How many female birds of this species arrive at the nesting area from $t = 15$ to $t = 45$? Show the setup for your calculations, and round your answer to the nearest integer.

Part D
On the interval $15 < t < 30$, the difference in the rates at which male and female birds of this species arrive at the nesting area can be modeled by the differentiable function $D(t)=M(t) - F(t)$, where $F$ is the function defined in part C. Is there a time $t$ in the interval $15 < t < 20$ when $D(t)=0$? Justify your answer.

Solution Block
Part A — Approximate M′(7.5)

Given: $M$ is differentiable on $[0,30]$; selected values as in the table.

Required: An approximation for $M'(7.5)$.

Since $M$ is differentiable, we approximate $M'(7.5)$ using the average rate of change over $[5,10]$, which contains $t=7.5$:

$$M'(7.5)\approx\frac{M(10)-M(5)}{10-5}=\frac{16-7}{5}=\frac{9}{5}$$
$M'(7.5)\approx 1.8$ birds per day per day
Part B(i) — Midpoint Riemann Sum

Subintervals $[0,10],[10,20],[20,30]$ each have width 10. Midpoints are $t=5,15,25$:

$$\int_0^{30}\!M(t)\,dt\approx 10\cdot M(5)+10\cdot M(15)+10\cdot M(25)=70+60+20$$
$\approx 150$ birds
Part B(ii) — Interpretation
$\displaystyle\int_0^{30}\!M(t)\,dt$ represents the total number of male birds that arrived at the nesting area over the 30-day period (in birds).
Part C — Total Female Birds from t = 15 to t = 45

Required: $\displaystyle\int_{15}^{45}F(t)\,dt$, rounded to the nearest integer.

$$\int_{15}^{45}\!\left[18+16\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{20}(t+15)\right)\right]dt$$

This is a calculator-active step (radian mode). Evaluating numerically:

$\approx 642$ female birds
Part D — Is there a time t in (15, 20) when D(t) = 0?

Given: $D(t)=M(t)-F(t)$. $M$ is differentiable and $F$ is continuous on $[15, 20]$, so $D$ is continuous on the closed interval $[15, 20]$.

We evaluate $D$ at the endpoints of the interval $[15, 20]$:

At $t=15$: $F(15)=18+16\sin\left(\frac{30\pi}{20}\right)=18+16(-1)=2$. Thus, $D(15)=M(15)-F(15)=6-2=4>0$.

At $t=20$: $F(20)=18+16\sin\left(\frac{35\pi}{20}\right)=18-8\sqrt{2}\approx 6.69$. Thus, $D(20)=M(20)-F(20)\approx 5-6.69=-1.69<0$.< /p>

Since $D(15)>0$ and $D(20)<0$, and $D$ is continuous on $[15,20]$, the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees at least one time $t$ in the open interval $15 < t < 20$ such that $D(t)=0$.

Yes, there is a time $t \in (15, 20)$ where $D(t) = 0$.

Problem 2: Polar Area & Curves

AP Exam Question — As Written

Let $S$ be the shaded region bounded by the graph of the polar curve $r(\theta) = 3 + 2\sin(2\theta) + \cos(2\theta)$ for $0 \le \theta \le \pi$, as shown in the figure.

Figure — Interactive Rendering

It can be shown that $r'(\theta) = 4\cos(2\theta) - 2\sin(2\theta)$.
(Note: Your calculator should be in radian mode.)

Part A
Find the area of region $S$. Show the setup for your calculations.

Part B
There is a point on the curve at which the slope of the line tangent to the curve is $-\dfrac{3}{7}$. At this point, $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = \dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}$. Find $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta}$ at this point. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Part C
i. Find the value of $\theta$ in the interval $0 < \theta < \dfrac{\pi}{2}$ at which $r$ has a critical point.
ii. Use a derivative test to determine whether the critical point is the location of a relative minimum, a relative maximum, or neither for $r$.

Part D
Find the average distance from the origin to a point on the polar curve $r(\theta) = 3 + 2\sin(2\theta) + \cos(2\theta)$ for $\dfrac{\pi}{2} \le \theta \le \pi$. Show the setup for your calculations.

Solution Block
Part A — Area of S

Given: $r(\theta)=3+2\sin(2\theta)+\cos(2\theta)$, $0\le\theta\le\pi$.

Applying the polar area formula:

$$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi}\!\bigl[r(\theta)\bigr]^2\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi}\!\bigl[3+2\sin(2\theta)+\cos(2\theta)\bigr]^2d\theta$$
[Evaluate on graphing calculator in radian mode]
Part B — Find dx/dθ at the point where dy/dx = −3/7

Given: $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta}=\dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}$ and $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{3}{7}$ at the required point.

We recall that for polar curves: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}$. Rearranging:

$$\frac{dx}{d\theta}=\frac{dy/d\theta}{dy/dx}=\frac{3\sqrt{2}/2}{-3/7}=\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\cdot\frac{-7}{3}$$
$\dfrac{dx}{d\theta}=-\dfrac{7\sqrt{2}}{2}$
Part C — Critical point of r on (0, π/2)

We are given $r'(\theta)=4\cos(2\theta)-2\sin(2\theta)$. Setting $r'(\theta)=0$:

$$4\cos(2\theta)=2\sin(2\theta)\implies\tan(2\theta)=2$$

In $0<\theta<\pi /2$: $2\theta=\arctan 2$, so $\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}\arctan 2\approx 0.5536$ rad.

Since $r''$ is negative at this point (verify by calculator), $r$ has a relative maximum there.

$\theta=\tfrac{1}{2}\arctan 2$ — relative maximum of $r$
Part D — Average distance from origin for π/2 ≤ θ ≤ π
$$\bar{r}=\frac{1}{\pi/2}\int_{\pi/2}^{\pi}r(\theta)\,d\theta=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_{\pi/2}^{\pi}\!\bigl[3+2\sin(2\theta)+\cos(2\theta)\bigr]d\theta$$
[Evaluate on calculator]

Problem 3: Differential Equations

AP Exam Question — As Written

A pie is taken from a hot oven and put on a table. The internal temperature of the pie at time $t$ minutes can be modeled by the function $H$ that satisfies the differential equation $\dfrac{dH}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{15}(H - 20)$, where $H(t)$ is measured in degrees Celsius and $H(0) = 75$. For $t > 0$, it is known that $20 < H(t) < 75$.

Part A
Explain why the following could not be a slope field for the differential equation $\dfrac{dH}{dt} = -\dfrac{1}{15}(H - 20)$.

Figure — Interactive Rendering

Part B
Find the slope of the line tangent to the graph of $H$ at time $t = 0$. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Part C
It can be shown that $\dfrac{d^2H}{dt^2} = \dfrac{1}{225}(H - 20)$. The line tangent to the graph of $H$ at time $t = 0$ is used to approximate $H(5)$, the internal temperature of the pie at time $t = 5$. Is this approximation an overestimate or an underestimate for the actual value of $H(5)$? Give a reason for your answer.

Part D
Use separation of variables to find an expression for $H(t)$, the particular solution to the given differential equation with initial condition $H(0) = 75$.

Solution Block
Part A — Why the given slope field is incorrect

Observation: It is given that $20 < H(t) < 75$ for $t>0$. Therefore $H-20>0$ throughout.

Substituting into the differential equation:

$$\frac{dH}{dt}=-\frac{1}{15}\underbrace{(H-20)}_{>0}<0$$

Hence all slope segments must be negative — tilting downward to the right. The given slope field shows exclusively positive slopes, which directly contradicts this. Therefore it cannot be a slope field for this differential equation.

Part B — Slope of tangent line at t = 0

Given: $H(0)=75$. Required: $\left.\dfrac{dH}{dt}\right|_{t=0}$.

Substituting directly into the differential equation:

$$\left.\frac{dH}{dt}\right|_{t=0}=-\frac{1}{15}(H(0)-20)=-\frac{1}{15}(75-20)=-\frac{55}{15}$$
Slope $=-\dfrac{11}{3}$

Note: On the non-calculator section, leave the answer as $-55/15$ or $-11/3$. Do not convert to decimal.

Part C — Is the tangent line approximation of H(5) an overestimate or underestimate?

Given: $\dfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}=\dfrac{1}{225}(H-20)$.

Since $H>20$, we have $H-20>0$, and therefore $\dfrac{d^2H}{dt^2}>0$. The graph of $H$ is concave up.

For a concave up function, every tangent line lies below the curve at all points away from the point of tangency. Therefore the tangent line gives a value less than the actual $H(5)$.

The approximation is an underestimate.
Part D — Solve by Separation of Variables

Required: Find $H(t)$ satisfying the DE with $H(0)=75$.

Step 1 — Separate variables. Place all $H$-terms on the left, all $t$-terms on the right: $$\frac{dH}{H-20}=-\frac{1}{15}\,dt$$ Step 2 — Integrate both sides. $$\int\frac{dH}{H-20}=\int-\frac{1}{15}\,dt\implies\ln|H-20|=-\frac{t}{15}+C_1$$ Step 3 — Exponentiate both sides. $$|H-20|=e^{C_1}\cdot e^{-t/15}$$ Step 4 — Write $A=\pm e^{C_1}$ (absorbing the absolute value; $A$ may be any nonzero constant): $$H(t)=Ae^{-t/15}+20$$ Step 5 — Apply initial condition $H(0)=75$. $$75=A\cdot e^{0}+20\implies A=55$$
$H(t)=55\,e^{-t/15}+20$

Problem 4: Graph of f'

AP Exam Question — As Written

Let $f$ be a twice-differentiable function on the closed interval $[-4, 4]$ with $f(2) = 3$. The graph of $f'$, the derivative of $f$, is shown.

Figure — Interactive Rendering

Part A
For $x > 0$, the function $g$ is defined by $g(x) = f(x) - \ln x$. Find $g'(2)$. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Part B
Find all values of $x$ on the open interval $0 < x < 3$ at which the graph of $f$ has a point of inflection. Give a reason for your answer.

Part C
For $-4 \le x \le 4$, on what open intervals, if any, is the graph of $f$ both increasing and concave down? Give a reason for your answer.

Part D
For $-4 \le x \le 4$, find the value of $x$ at which $f$ has an absolute minimum and the value of $x$ at which $f$ has an absolute maximum. Give reasons for your answers.

Solution Block
Part A — Find g′(2) where g(x) = f(x) − ln x

Given: $g(x)=f(x)-\ln x$ for $x>0$; from the graph, $f'(2)=1.5$.

Differentiating: $g'(x)=f'(x)-\dfrac{1}{x}$. Evaluating at $x=2$:

$$g'(2)=f'(2)-\frac{1}{2}=1.5-0.5=1$$
$g'(2)=1$
Part B — Points of inflection of f on (0, 3)

Required: $x\in(0,3)$ where $f$ has an inflection point.

Recall: $f$ has an inflection point where $f''$ changes sign, i.e., where $f'$ has a local extremum.

From the graph, $f'$ attains a local maximum at $x=1$ (where $f'$ changes from increasing to decreasing). Therefore $f''$ changes sign from $+$ to $-$ at $x=1$.

Inflection point at $x=1$; $f''$ changes sign from $+$ to $-$ there.
Part C — Intervals where f is increasing and concave down

$f$ increasing $\Leftrightarrow$ $f'(x)>0$. From graph: $f'>0$ on $(-2,\,3)$.

$f$ concave down $\Leftrightarrow$ $f''(x)<0$ $\Leftrightarrow$ $f'$ is decreasing. From graph: $f'$ decreasing on $(1,\,3)$.

Intersection of both conditions:

$f$ is increasing and concave down on $(1,\,3)$.
Part D — Absolute minimum and maximum of f on [−4, 4]

Candidates for absolute extrema occur at the endpoints ($x = -4, 4$) and critical points where $f'(x) = 0$ ($x = -2, 3$).

Absolute Minimum: Since $f'(x) < 0$ on $[-4, -2]$, $f$ is strictly decreasing on this interval, meaning $f(-2) < f(-4)$. On $[-2, 3]$, $f'(x)> 0$, so $f$ is strictly increasing. On $[3, 4]$, the graph shows $f'$ becomes negative briefly but then turns strongly positive up to $f'(4) = 5$. The total net area accumulated from $x = -2$ to $x = 4$ is positive, making $x = -2$ the global low point.

Absolute Maximum: Since $f$ increases over the broad interval $[-2, 3]$ and finishes with a dramatic positive rate toward $f'(4) = 5$, the absolute maximum occurs at the right endpoint where the net accumulated area under $f'$ is greatest.

Absolute minimum at $x = -2$; Absolute maximum at $x = 4$.

Problem 5: Area & Volume

AP Exam Question — As Written

Let $f$ be the function defined by $f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x - 1}$, and let $g$ be the function defined by $g(x) = e^{(-2x + 4)}$. The graphs of $f$ and $g$ intersect at the point $(2, 1)$. Let $R$ be the region bounded by the graph of $f$, the $x$-axis, and the vertical line $x = 2$, as shown in the figure.

Figure — Interactive Rendering

Part A
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^2 f(x)\,dx$. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Part B
Write, but do not evaluate, an expression involving one or more integrals that gives the volume of the solid generated when region $R$ is rotated about the $x$-axis.

Part C
Write, but do not evaluate, an expression involving one or more integrals that gives the perimeter of region $R$.

Part D
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_2^{\infty} g(x)\,dx$. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Solution Block
Part A — Evaluate ∫₁² f(x) dx
Step 1 — Apply the power rule. $$\int(x-1)^{1/3}\,dx=\frac{(x-1)^{4/3}}{4/3}+C=\frac{3}{4}(x-1)^{4/3}+C$$ Step 2 — Evaluate at the limits. $$\left[\frac{3}{4}(x-1)^{4/3}\right]_1^2=\frac{3}{4}(1)^{4/3}-\frac{3}{4}(0)^{4/3}=\frac{3}{4}-0$$
$\displaystyle\int_1^2(x-1)^{1/3}\,dx=\dfrac{3}{4}$
Part B — Volume when R is rotated about the x-axis (write, do not evaluate)

By the disk method, cross-sections perpendicular to the $x$-axis are disks of radius $f(x)=(x-1)^{1/3}$:

$V=\pi\displaystyle\int_1^2(x-1)^{2/3}\,dx$
Part C — Perimeter of R (write, do not evaluate)

The boundary of $R$ has three parts: the curve $y=f(x)$ from $x=1$ to $x=2$, the vertical segment $x=2$ from $y=0$ to $y=1$, and the segment along $x$-axis from $x=1$ to $x=2$:

$$\text{Perimeter}=\int_1^2\!\sqrt{1+\bigl[f'(x)\bigr]^2}\,dx\;+\;1\;+\;1$$

where $f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{3}(x-1)^{-2/3}$.

Part D — Evaluate ∫₂^∞ g(x) dx (Improper Integral)
Step 1 — Write as a limit. $$\int_2^{\infty}e^{-2x+4}\,dx=\lim_{b\to\infty}\int_2^b e^{-2x+4}\,dx$$ Step 2 — Evaluate the definite integral. $$=\lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-\frac{1}{2}e^{-2x+4}\right]_2^b=\lim_{b\to\infty}\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}e^{-2b+4}+\frac{1}{2}e^{0}\right)$$ Step 3 — Take the limit. As $b\to\infty$, $e^{-2b+4}\to 0$: $$=0+\frac{1}{2}$$
$\displaystyle\int_2^{\infty}e^{-2x+4}\,dx=\dfrac{1}{2}$

Problem 6: Series & Error Bound

AP Exam Question — As Written

The Maclaurin series for a function $g$ is given by

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 2\left(\frac{-x}{5}\right)^n = 2 - \frac{2}{5}x + \frac{2}{25}x^2 - \frac{2}{125}x^3 + \frac{2}{625}x^4 - \cdots + 2\left(\frac{-x}{5}\right)^n + \cdots$$

and converges to $g(x)$ for $|x| < 5$.

Part A
For $x > 0$, the Maclaurin series for $g$ is an alternating geometric series. Find $g(3)$. Show the work that leads to your answer.

Part B
The function $f$ is defined by $f(x) = g'(x)$. Write the first four nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $f$.

Part C
The second-degree Taylor polynomial for $f$ about $x = 0$ is used to approximate $f\!\left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)$ as $-\dfrac{3}{10}$, where $f$ is the function defined in part B. Justify that $\left|f\!\left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right) - \left(-\dfrac{3}{10}\right)\right| \le \dfrac{1}{5}$.

Part D
The function $h$ is defined by $h(x) = 25g(x) - 2e^x$.
i. Write the first three nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $e^x$.
ii. Write the first three nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $h$.

Solution Block
Part A — Find g(3)

We identify: first term $a=2$, common ratio $r=-x/5$. At $x=3$: $r=-3/5$, $|r|<1$ ✓. By the infinite geometric series formula:

$$g(3)=\frac{a}{1-r}=\frac{2}{1+3/5}=\frac{2}{8/5}=\frac{10}{8}$$
$g(3)=\dfrac{5}{4}$
Part B — First four nonzero terms of f(x) = g′(x)

Differentiating the series for $g(x)$ term by term:

$$f(x)=g'(x)=-\frac{2}{5}+\frac{4}{25}x-\frac{6}{125}x^2+\frac{8}{625}x^3-\cdots$$
$f(x)=-\dfrac{2}{5}+\dfrac{4}{25}x-\dfrac{6}{125}x^2+\dfrac{8}{625}x^3$
Part C — Justify the error bound |f(5/2) − (−3/10)| ≤ 1/5

The second-degree Taylor polynomial is $P_2(x)=-\dfrac{2}{5}+\dfrac{4}{25}x-\dfrac{6}{125}x^2$.

At $x=5/2$: $P_2\!\left(\tfrac{5}{2}\right)=-\tfrac{2}{5}+\tfrac{2}{5}-\tfrac{3}{10}=-\tfrac{3}{10}$ ✓

Since the series for $f$ is alternating with terms decreasing in absolute value at $x=5/2$, the Alternating Series Error Bound gives:

$$\left|f\!\left(\tfrac{5}{2}\right)-P_2\!\left(\tfrac{5}{2}\right)\right|\le\left|\frac{8}{625}\cdot\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^3\right|=\frac{8}{625}\cdot\frac{125}{8}=\frac{125}{625}$$
Error $\le\dfrac{1}{5}$ $\checkmark$
Part D — Maclaurin series for h(x) = 25g(x) − 2eˣ

Part D(i) — First three nonzero terms of $e^x$:

$e^x=1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\cdots$

Part D(ii) — First three nonzero terms of $h(x)$:

Step 1: $25g(x)=25\!\left(2-\dfrac{2}{5}x+\dfrac{2}{25}x^2-\cdots\right)=50-10x+2x^2-\cdots$ Step 2: $2e^x=2\!\left(1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\cdots\right)=2+2x+x^2+\cdots$ Step 3 — Subtract term by term: $$h(x)=(50-2)+(-10-2)x+(2-1)x^2+\cdots=48-12x+x^2+\cdots$$
$h(x)=48-12x+x^2+\cdots$
