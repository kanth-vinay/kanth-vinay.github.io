12. Depreciation of Inventory
Model + Extremum
Suppose the value \(V\) (in thousands of dollars) at Fido’s Pet Supply after \(t\) months is \[ V(t)=40-\frac{25t^2}{(t+2)^2}. \] (a) Compute \(V(0),V(5),V(10),V(70)\). (b) Find the maximum of \(V\) on \([0,\infty)\). (c) Sketch \(V\). (d) Is there a floor value below which \(V(t)\) never falls?
Hints

(a) Substitute the given values of \(t\) directly into \(V(t)=40-\dfrac{25t^2}{(t+2)^2}\).

(b) Differentiate using the Quotient Rule on \(\dfrac{25t^2}{(t+2)^2}\): if \(u=25t^2\) and \(v=(t+2)^2\), then \(\dfrac{d}{dt}\!\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)=\dfrac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}\). Solve \(V'(t)=0\) for critical points in \([0,\infty)\), then compare \(V\) at those points and at the endpoint \(t=0\).

(c) Use the sign chart of \(V'(t)\) and key features (horizontal asymptote and intercept) to sketch the curve.

(d) Find the long-run floor by computing \(\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} V(t)=40-25\,\lim_{t\to\infty}\dfrac{t^2}{(t+2)^2}\). Since numerator and denominator have the same degree, the fraction tends to 1.

13. Pollution-Control Costs
Rational Model + Domain
Cost to remove \(p\%\) of pollutants: \[ C(p)=\frac{48{,}000}{100-p}. \] (a) Compute \(C(0),C(15),C(60),C(90)\). (b) Domain of \(C\). (c) Sketch \(C\). (d) Discuss feasibility of \(p=100\%\).
Hints

(a) Evaluate by direct substitution: \(C(0), C(15), C(60), C(90)\).

(b) Practical domain is \(0\le p<100\). There is a vertical asymptote at \(p=100\) because the denominator is zero.

(c) The function is decreasing and convex. As \(p\to100^-\), the cost grows without bound.

(d) Discuss \(\displaystyle \lim_{p\to100^-} C(p)\) to explain why 100% removal is economically infeasible in this model.

14. Medication Decay
Decay + Asymptote
With \(A_0=120\) cc, the bloodstream amount is \[ A(t)=\frac{A_0}{t^2+1}. \] (a) Compute \(A(0),A(1),A(2),A(8),A(10)\). (b) Max of \(A\) on \([0,\infty)\). (c) Sketch \(A\). (d) Does medicine ever fully leave the bloodstream?
Hints

(a) Substitute the listed \(t\)-values directly into \(A(t)\).

(b) Differentiate \(A(t)\); set \(A'(t)=0\) to confirm the maximum occurs at \(t=0\).

(c) Use the sign of \(A'(t)\) together with the horizontal asymptote \(y=0\) to guide the sketch.

(d) Evaluate \(\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty}A(t)\) to decide whether the medicine ever fully leaves the bloodstream.

34. Roped-Off Swimming Area
Geometric Max Area
A lifeguard ropes off a rectangular swimming area along a straight shoreline using 2300 yards of rope for only three sides (the shoreline forms the fourth side). Determine the dimensions that maximize the area, and report the maximum area and the corresponding dimensions.
Hints

What you are modeling: A rectangle whose side along the shoreline uses no rope. The two sides perpendicular to shore are \(x\) and \(x\); the side parallel to shore is \(L\). Only those three sides use rope.

Let sides be \(x,x\) perpendicular to shore and \(L\) along shore. Constraint \(2x+L=2300\Rightarrow L=2300-2x\). Area \(A(x)=x(2300-2x)\).

35. Open Box from 29×16
Maximize Volume
From a 29-by-16 rectangular sheet, identical squares of side \(x\) are cut from each corner and the sides are folded up to create an open-top box. Find the value of \(x\) that maximizes the volume, and report the resulting dimensions and maximum volume.
Hints

What happens when you cut squares? Cutting out a square of side \(x\) from each corner and folding up gives height \(h=x\), length \(29-2x\), and width \(16-2x\).

The volume is \(V(x)=x(29-2x)(16-2x)\). The practical domain is \(0<x<8\) because you cannot cut more than half the 16-unit side.

Differentiate \(V(x)\), set \(V'(x)=0\) to find an interior critical point, and compare with endpoints from the domain.

36. Dumpster with Min Surface Area
Constrained Minimum
A dumpster has an open top. Its length is twice its width, and its volume is \(30\,\text{yd}^3\). Find the dimensions that minimize the surface area of material used.
Hints

Goal: Use the volume to express height in terms of width, then write the surface area (bottom + four sides; no top) so you can minimize a single function of \(x\).

Let width \(x\), length \(2x\), height \(h\). Constraint \(2x^2h=30\Rightarrow h=\frac{15}{x^2}\). SA (no top): \(S(x)=2x^2+6xh=2x^2+\frac{90}{x}\).

37. Profit Maximization
Profit = Revenue - Cost
\(R(x)=60x-0.5x^2,\; C(x)=4x+10.\) Find \(x\) that maximizes profit and the profit.
Hints

Profit is always Revenue minus Cost. Here, \(P(x)=R(x)-C(x)=(60x-0.5x^2)-(4x+10)=-0.5x^2+56x-10\). Find the maximum either by the vertex (downward parabola) or by solving \(P'(x)=0\).

38. Profit (Thousands of Units)
Profit = Revenue - Cost
\(R(x)=9x-3x^2,\; C(x)=x^2-5x+2x^2+13.\) Find production level and maximum profit.
Hints

First simplify the cost: \(C(x)=x^2-5x+2x^2+13=3x^2-5x+13\). Then Profit \(P(x)=R(x)-C(x)=(9x-3x^2)-(3x^2-5x+13)=-6x^2+14x-13\). Optimize via the vertex or \(P'(x)=0\).

39. Orchard Density
Quadratic Yield
An orchard currently has 19 trees per acre and yields 33 bushels per tree. For each additional tree added per acre, the yield per tree decreases by 1 bushel. How many trees per acre should be planted to maximize the total yield?
Hints

Yield/tree \(=52-n\). Total \(Y(n)=n(52-n)\). Vertex gives optimal \(n\).

40. Cost-Minimizing Box (Square Base & Top)
Cost Model
A box has a square base and a square top with total volume \(240\text{ ft}^3\). The bottom costs \$0.15 per square foot, the top costs \$0.10 per square foot, and the sides cost \$0.015 per square foot. Find the dimensions that minimize the total construction cost.
Hints

Why the costs look like this: bottom and top each have area \(x^2\) but different prices; four sides have total area \(4xh\). The volume constraint \(x^2h=240\) lets you eliminate \(h\).

Let base side \(x\), height \(h\). \(x^2h=240\Rightarrow h=240/x^2\). \(C(x)=0.25x^2+0.06xh=0.25x^2+\frac{14.4}{x}\).

41. Inventory (EOQ with Per-Order Cost)
Economic Order Quantity
Demand \(Q=600\) units/yr, holding \(d=\$2\)/unit-yr, fixed order cost \(b=\$6\), per-unit order fee \$2.75 (doesn’t affect EOQ). Find orders/year and lot size to minimize inventory cost.
Hints

What EOQ balances: ordering less often saves on order fees but increases average on‑hand inventory; ordering very often does the opposite.

The EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) balances two forces: ordering less often (fewer orders ⇒ lower ordering cost) vs. keeping bigger lots on shelves (more inventory ⇒ higher holding cost). A per-unit purchase fee does not affect this balance, so we ignore the $2.75 here.

1) Lot size (EOQ): \(q^*=\sqrt{\tfrac{2bQ}{d}}\).

2) Orders per year: \(\dfrac{Q}{q^*}=\sqrt{\tfrac{Qd}{2b}}\).

Now plug in \(Q=600\), \(d=2\), \(b=6\) to get numbers. (Units: \(q^*\) in units/order; \(Q/q^*\) in orders/year.)

42. Open-Top Cylinder (Min SA)
Calculus Minimum
Design an open-top circular cylinder with volume \(V=1728\ \text{cm}^3\) that minimizes surface area. Find the optimal radius and height.
Hints

Open top means: only one circular base and the curved side count toward surface area. Use the volume constraint to solve for \(h\) in terms of \(r\), then substitute into \(S(r)\).

\(h=\frac{1728}{\pi r^2}\). \(S(r)=\pi r^2+2\pi r h=\pi r^2+\frac{3456}{r}\). Set \(S'(r)=0\).

44. Power Line Across Water (Cost Min)
Snell-type Tradeoff
Island \(C\) is 5 mi offshore from \(A\); \(B\) is 8 mi downshore from \(A\). Underwater \(\$5400/\text{mi}\), land \(\$3000/\text{mi}\). Find the shore point \(S\) (distance \(x=\overline{AS}\) from \(A\)) that minimizes total cost.
Hints

Underwater distance \(CS=\sqrt{25+x^2}\). Land distance \(SB=8-x\).

\[ C(x)=5400\sqrt{25+x^2}+3000\,(8-x),\qquad 0\le x\le 8. \]

\[ C'(x)=5400\cdot\frac{x}{\sqrt{25+x^2}}-3000.\quad \text{Set }C'(x)=0\text{ and compare endpoints.} \]

45. Homing Pigeon (Energy Min)
Weighted Distance
\(C\) is 10 mi offshore from the shore point 22 mi from \(A\). Water energy rate \(=1.35\times\) land. Let \(x=\overline{AS}\) along shore toward that point. Find where to land \(S\) to minimize energy to \(A\).
Hints

Picture: The bird starts at \(C\) (10 miles offshore from the point opposite \(B\)), lands at some point \(S\) between \(A\) and \(B\) on the shore, then runs to \(A\). Water miles cost 1.35 times land miles.

Goal: pick a landing point \(S\) that balances energy over water (costlier per mile) with energy over land (cheaper). The best \(S\) is where the rate of change of total energy is zero.

Water leg \(CS=\sqrt{100+(22-x)^2}\), land leg \(SA=x\).

\[ E(x)=1.35\sqrt{100+(22-x)^2}+x,\qquad 0\le x\le 22. \] Differentiate and solve \(E'(x)=0\); compare endpoints if needed.

46. Bridge Placement Between Two Cities
General Model
Cities \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) lie on opposite sides of a river of width \(r\). City \(C_1\) is \(a\) from the river, city \(C_2\) is \(b\) from the river, and their along-river separation is \(p\) with \(a\le b\). Determine where to place a perpendicular bridge to minimize the total travel distance.
Hints

\[ D(x)=\sqrt{a^2+x^2}+r+\sqrt{b^2+(p-x)^2},\quad D'(x)=\frac{x}{\sqrt{a^2+x^2}}-\frac{p-x}{\sqrt{b^2+(p-x)^2}}. \] Solve \(D'(x)=0\) (Snell-like balance).

47. EOQ — General & Example
Closed-Form
Annual demand \(Q\), holding \(d\), order cost \(b\). (Per-unit purchase cost \(c\) doesn’t change EOQ.) Find orders/year and lot size that minimize annual inventory cost. Then apply to \(Q=100,d=\$18,b=\$36,c=\$11\).
Hints

Reminder: EOQ formulas minimize the sum of ordering + holding cost; the unit purchase price \(c\) does not appear in \(q^*\).

Use the EOQ closed form to minimize annual inventory cost (ordering + holding). The unit price \(c\) cancels out for EOQ itself.

General: Lot size \(q^*=\sqrt{\tfrac{2bQ}{d}}\); orders/year \(=\dfrac{Q}{q^*}=\sqrt{\tfrac{Qd}{2b}}\).

Example: With \(Q=100\), \(d=18\), \(b=36\) (and \(c=11\) ignored for EOQ), compute \(q^*\), then \(Q/q^*\). Report the rounded lot size and orders/year.
