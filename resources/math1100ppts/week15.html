    Week 15

    Part 1: The D-Test (Second Derivative Test)

    Finding the "Peaks and Valleys"

    For a function of two variables \(f(x,y)\), a critical point \((a,b)\) occurs where:

    \( f_x = 0 \)    and    \( f_y = 0 \)

    Just like setting \(f'(x)=0\) in Calculus I, we now set both partial derivatives to zero simultaneously to find points where the tangent plane is horizontal.

    Wait! Is it a max, a min, or neither? We need the Second Derivative Test.

    Visualizing the Tangent Lines

    At a peak (Max) or valley (Min), the tangent lines in both the \(x\) and \(y\) directions are perfectly horizontal. They intersect to form a flat tangent plane.

    The D-Test Formula

    \( \displaystyle D = f_{xx}(a,b) \cdot f_{yy}(a,b) - [f_{xy}(a,b)]^2 \)
    • If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\)   \(\implies\)   Relative Maximum
    • If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} > 0\)   \(\implies\)   Relative Minimum
    • If \(D < 0\)   \(\implies\)   Saddle Point (neither max nor min)
    • If \(D = 0\)   \(\implies\)   Test is inconclusive.

    Visualizing Max, Min, and Saddle Points

    Interact with the 3D surfaces below to see what \(D > 0\) vs \(D < 0\) looks like.

    Paraboloid (Min)
    \(z = x^2 + y^2\)

    \(D = (2)(2) - 0^2 = 4 > 0\)

    Saddle Point
    \(z = x^2 - y^2\)

    \(D = (2)(-2) - 0^2 = -4 < 0\)

    Think-Pair-Share

    Suppose you find a critical point at \((2, -1)\) for a function \(f(x,y)\). You calculate the second partials and find:

    • \(f_{xx}(2,-1) = -4\)
    • \(f_{yy}(2,-1) = -2\)
    • \(f_{xy}(2,-1) = 3\)

    Calculate \(D\). What type of point is \((2, -1)\)?

    Part 2: Constrained Optimization

    Sometimes we don't want the absolute maximum of a function everywhere. We only want the maximum subject to a specific condition or constraint.

    • Objective Function: What we want to maximize/minimize (e.g., Profit, Area).
      \(f(x,y) = xy\)
    • Constraint: The rule we must follow (e.g., Budget, Perimeter).
      \(g(x,y) = x + y - 20 = 0\)
    HW-11 Connection: This is exactly like Problem #9, where you minimize Cost subject to producing a fixed volume of solar panels!

    The Method of Lagrange Multipliers

    To optimize \(f(x,y)\) subject to \(g(x,y) = 0\):

    1. Form the Lagrange Function:
      \( F(x,y,\lambda) = f(x,y) - \lambda \cdot g(x,y) \)
    2. Find the Partial Derivatives: Compute \(F_x, F_y,\) and \(F_\lambda\).
    3. Solve the System:
      • \(F_x = 0\)
      • \(F_y = 0\)
      • \(F_\lambda = 0\)   (This is simply your constraint equation \(g(x,y)=0\))

    Intuition: Tangent Contours

    The maximum occurs where the constraint curve is exactly tangent to a contour line (level curve) of the objective function.

    Example: Maximizing Area \(A = xy\) subject to perimeter constraint \(x + y = 20\).

    The red line is your budget/perimeter. You walk along it until you hit the highest possible "elevation" contour line (yellow).

    How do we know if it's a Min or Max?

    1. The "Plug It In" Method

    Unlike the D-Test, we don't have a simple formula. If you find multiple critical points, just plug them all into the objective function \(f(x,y)\).

    • The largest value is your Maximum.
    • The smallest value is your Minimum.

    2. Context Clues

    In many business problems, you will only find one critical point.

    • If the problem asks to minimize Cost, that single point is your minimum.
    • If it asks to maximize Profit, it's your maximum.
    Trust the setup of the word problem!

    Worked Example: Cylinder Surface Area

    Objective: Minimize surface area \(S(r,h) = 2\pi rh + 2\pi r^2\).

    Constraint: Volume is fixed at \(21.66\).
    \(g(r,h) = \pi r^2 h - 21.66 = 0\).

    Lagrange Function:
    \(F(r,h,\lambda) = 2\pi rh + 2\pi r^2 - \lambda(\pi r^2 h - 21.66)\)

    The System:

    1. \(F_r = 2\pi h + 4\pi r - \lambda (2\pi rh) = 0\)
    2. \(F_h = 2\pi r - \lambda (\pi r^2) = 0\)
    3. \(F_\lambda = -(\pi r^2 h - 21.66) = 0\)

    From (2): \(2\pi r = \lambda \pi r^2 \implies \lambda = \frac{2}{r}\).
    Substitute into (1) to find \(h = 2r\).

    Part 3: Extreme Value Theorem

    If \(f(x,y)\) is continuous on a closed and bounded region, it must have an absolute maximum and minimum in that region.

    Where to look?

    1. Interior Critical Points: Where \(f_x = 0\) and \(f_y = 0\) (using the D-Test concepts).
    2. Boundaries: Optimize along the edges (often reduces to a Calc I problem or a Lagrange problem).
    3. Vertices/Corners: Evaluate \(f(x,y)\) at the intersection of boundary lines.
    Common Pitfall: Finding an interior critical point and stopping. The absolute max/min is very often sitting on the boundary line!

    Homework 11 Strategy Guide

    Questions 1-7: The D-Test

    Unconstrained optimization.

    1. Find \(f_x\) and \(f_y\).
    2. Set both to 0 and solve for \((x,y)\).
    3. Calculate \(D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2\).
    4. Classify: Max, Min, or Saddle.

    Questions 8-25: Lagrange / Apps

    Constrained optimization. Look for the hidden constraint!

    • "Budget constraint" \(\implies g(x,y) = Cost - Budget = 0\)
    • "Fixed perimeter" \(\implies g(x,y) = Perimeter - C = 0\)
    • "Make 150 units total" \(\implies x + y = 150\)

    Form \(F = f - \lambda g\) and solve.
