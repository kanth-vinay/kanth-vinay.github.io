    The Big Idea: "The Meeting Point"

    Imagine two friends, LEFTY and RIGHTY.

    They agree to meet at the Coffee Shop ($x=c$).

    The Limit Exists IF:
    They approach the SAME location.

    It DOES NOT matter if the shop is Open or Closed (Defined), only that they meet at the door.

    1.1 Mathematical Notation

    The Language

    Left Limit: $x \to c^-$

    Approaching from values slightly SMALLER than c

    Right Limit: $x \to c^+$

    Approaching from values slightly LARGER than c
    Official Definition:
    $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$

    Exists IF AND ONLY IF:
    $\text{Left Approach} = \text{Right Approach}$

    If they miss each other $\to$ DNE

    The "Wall" Method (Graphical)

    Goal: Use the graph to see if they meet.

    1. Draw a vertical "Wall" where $x = c$.
    2. Drive the car from the Left side $\to$ Crash into wall.
    3. Drive the car from the Right side $\to$ Crash into wall.
    4. Do the crash sites match?

    Example: Jump Discontinuity

    Numerical Approach (Using Tables)

    Why do tables?

    Sometimes we can't graph it easily, but we can plug in numbers.

    Strategy: Pick numbers getting closer and closer (0.1 away, then 0.01 away...).

    Piecewise Example

    $f(x) = \begin{cases} 2x & x < 2 \\ 4-x & x \ge 2 \end{cases}$

    Left ($2^-$): Plug in 1.9, 1.99 into $2x$.
    $\to 3.8, 3.98 \to$ 4

    Right ($2^+$): Plug in 2.1, 2.01 into $4-x$.
    $\to 1.9, 1.99 \to$ 2

    4 $\ne$ 2 (DNE)

    Rational Function ($x^2-9 / x-3$)

    What happens near 3?

    $x$ 2.9 2.99 $\to$ 3 $\leftarrow$ 3.01 3.1
    $f(x)$ 5.9 5.99 $\to$ 6 $\leftarrow$ 6.01 6.1

    Even though plugging in 3 breaks math (0/0), the neighbors agree on 6.

    Infinite Limits

    At a Point

    $f(x) = \frac{1}{x-3}$

    • Left ($2.9$): $\frac{1}{-0.1} \to -\infty$
    • Right ($3.1$): $\frac{1}{0.1} \to +\infty$
    DNE (Going apart)

    At Infinity

    $f(x) = \frac{3x+1}{x-2}$

    "Who wins?" Top or Bottom?

    Same power $\to$ Ratio of coefficients.

    3/1 = 3

    Vertical Asymptote

    If the Limit is $\infty$, that means there is an invisible vertical line the graph never touches.

    1.2 Algebraic Properties

    Direct Substitution

    The easiest way to find a limit.

    Just plug the number in!

    $\lim_{x \to 2} (x^2+1) = 2^2+1 = 5$

    (This works 90% of the time, unless you get 0/0).

    The Villain: 0/0

    If you plug in $c$ and get $\frac{0}{0}$, this is called Indeterminate.

    It means "I don't know yet, keep working".

    DO NOT WRITE "UNDEFINED" or "DNE"

    How to fight 0/0: Factoring

    Problem: $\lim_{x \to -3} \frac{x^2+x-6}{x+3}$

    1 Test It

    Plug in -3.

    $\frac{9-3-6}{-3+3} = \frac{0}{0}$

    Uh oh, it breaks.

    2 Factor It

    Top part factors into:

    $(x+3)(x-2)$

    See the $(x+3)$ on top and bottom?

    3 Cancel & Solve

    Kill the problem term.

    $\lim (x-2)$

    Now plug in -3 again.

    -3 - 2 = -5

    What was that 0/0?

    It was a "Hole"

    When terms cancel out, algebra puts a tiny hole in the graph.

    The path is smooth everywhere else.

    Our math simply filled in the pothole to find where the road would have gone.

    The Bridge Analogy

    A Safe Bridge Needs:

    1. Road Left exists.
    2. Road Right exists.
    3. Example: They align.
    4. The Middle Piece (plank) is nailed down exactly where the roads meet.

    Math Conditions:

    1. Limit Exists (Roads align).
    2. $f(c)$ is defined (Plank exists).
    3. Limit = $f(c)$ (Plank is in the right spot).

    Fixing the Bridge

    Find $k$ to make it continuous.

    $f(x) = \begin{cases} 3x + 4 & x < 2 \\ 2x + k & x \ge 2 \end{cases}$

    Left Road

    $3(2)+4 = 10$

    =

    Right Road

    $2(2)+k = 4+k$

    Force them to connect:

    $10 = 4 + k \implies k = 6$
