1. Initial Value Problems & Average Value

Initial Value Problems (IVPs)

When you take an indefinite integral, you generate a family of functions, denoted by the constant $+ C$. An Initial Value Problem provides a specific data point (e.g., $y(0) = 3$) allowing you to solve for $C$ and anchor the model to a specific physical reality.

Verifying Solutions: To prove a function is a solution to an equation like $y'' - \frac{1}{x} = 0$, you must calculate the required derivatives and substitute them algebraically to verify it equals zero.

Example: Solving an IVP

Problem: Find the particular solution for $y' = 8x^7$ given the initial condition $y(0) = 3$.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Step 1 (General Solution): Integrate to find the family of functions. $$ y = \int 8x^7 dx = 8 \left( \frac{x^8}{8} \right) + C = x^8 + C $$

Step 2 (Particular Solution): Apply the initial condition $y=3$ when $x=0$. $$ 3 = (0)^8 + C \implies C = 3 $$ The specific structural model is $y = x^8 + 3$.

Average Value of a Continuous Function

To find the average of a finite set of numbers, you sum them and divide by $n$. To find the average value of a continuous function over an interval $[a, b]$, you integrate to find the total "sum" (area) and divide by the width of the interval.

$$ f_{ave} = \frac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b f(x) dx $$

2. Differential Equations & Separation of Variables

A differential equation defines a continuous system by relating a function to its rate of change. We solve first-order DEs structurally by separating the variables: isolate all $y$ terms on the side with $dy$, and all $x$ terms on the side with $dx$, then integrate both sides independently.

Instructor Insight: Constant vs. Proportional Growth

Do not conflate a constant rate of change (linear models, $\frac{dy}{dt} = k$) with a constant percentage rate of change (exponential models, $\frac{dy}{dt} = ky$). The presence of the dependent variable $y$ in the derivative triggers exponential geometry. Furthermore, the ratio $\frac{A'(t)}{A(t)}$ structurally represents the continuous growth rate $k$.

Interactive: Continuous Growth Modeler

Adjust the initial value $C$ and the continuous rate $k$ for the system $\frac{dy}{dt} = ky$. Notice how negative rates instantly generate decay geometries.

Example: Separation of Variables (Algebraic)

Problem: Solve by separating variables: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 12x^{11}y$

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Step 1 (Separate): Divide by $y$ and multiply by $dx$. $$ \frac{1}{y} dy = 12x^{11} dx $$

Step 2 (Integrate): Integrate both sides. $$ \ln|y| = 12 \left(\frac{x^{12}}{12}\right) + C $$ $$ \ln|y| = x^{12} + C $$

Step 3 (Isolate y): Exponentiate both sides. Let $e^C$ become a new constant $C_1$. $$ y = e^{x^{12} + C} \implies y = Ce^{x^{12}} $$

Biology Application: The Weber-Fechner Law

Problem: The Weber-Fechner Law of stimulus-response models the DE $\frac{dR}{dS} = k \frac{1}{S}$, where $R$ is response and $S$ is stimulus. Solve for the general solution.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Step 1 (Separate): Isolate differentials with their respective variables: $$ dR = k \frac{1}{S} dS $$

Step 2 (Integrate): Integrate both sides independently: $$ \int dR = \int k \frac{1}{S} dS $$ $$ R(S) = k \ln|S| + C $$

Interpretation: The biological response magnitude is logarithmic with respect to stimulus intensity. This is why human hearing (decibels) operates on a log scale.

Business Application: Elasticity of Demand Differential

Problem: Find the demand function $q = D(x)$ given the elasticity condition $E(x) = \frac{x}{190-x}$ and the initial condition $q = 160$ when $x = 30$.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Step 1: Recall structural elasticity: $E = -\frac{x}{q} \frac{dq}{dx}$.

Step 2 (Separate): Set equations equal: $$ -\frac{x}{q} \frac{dq}{dx} = \frac{x}{190-x} \implies \frac{1}{q} dq = -\frac{1}{190-x} dx $$

Step 3 (Integrate): $$ \ln|q| = \ln|190-x| + C \implies q = K(190-x) $$

Step 4 (Initial Value): Evaluate at $x=30, q=160$: $$ 160 = K(190-30) \implies 160 = 160K \implies K=1 $$
Thus, the demand function is $q = 190 - x$.

3. Multivariable Domains & Evaluation

Evaluating and Constraining 3D Systems

A multivariable function $z = f(x,y)$ assigns an output to an ordered pair. To evaluate $f(5,-7)$, substitute 5 for every $x$ and -7 for every $y$.

The domain of $f(x,y)$ operates under the exact same structural constraints as single-variable calculus, but the result is a 2D geometric region in the $xy$-plane:

  • Denominators: Cannot equal zero (e.g., $2y - x^2 \ne 0$).
  • Radicands (Even Roots): Must be $\ge 0$ (e.g., $y - 8x \ge 0$).
Example: Domain Constraint Analysis

Problem: Determine the domain of the function $g(x,y) = \frac{7}{2y - x^2}$. Describe the geometric restriction.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Constraint Setup: The denominator cannot equal zero. $$ 2y - x^2 \neq 0 $$

Algebraic Isolation: Solve for $y$ to establish the boundary: $$ 2y \neq x^2 \implies y \neq \frac{1}{2}x^2 $$

Geometric Interpretation: The domain is $\{(x,y) | y \neq \frac{1}{2}x^2 \}$. This represents all coordinate points in the 2D plane except those lying exactly on the parabola.

Biology Example: Surface Area Evaluation

Problem: The Mosteller formula for approximating human surface area is $S(h,w) = \frac{\sqrt{hw}}{60}$ (height in cm, weight in kg). If a person's weight drops by 20%, by what percentage does their surface area change?

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Step 1 (Substitute): A 20% drop in weight means the new weight is $0.8w$. $$ S_{new} = \frac{\sqrt{h(0.8w)}}{60} = \sqrt{0.8} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{hw}}{60} $$

Step 2 (Analyze): Since $\sqrt{0.8} \approx 0.894$, the new surface area is $89.4\%$ of the original.

Conclusion: The surface area drops by approximately 10%.

4. Partial Derivatives & Marginal Analysis

A partial derivative measures the rate of change with respect to one specific variable while treating all other variables as static constants. Geometrically, this slices the 3D surface with a plane, resulting in a 2D curve, and finds the tangent slope of that curve.

Derivative TypeNotationOperation
First-Order (w.r.t $x$)$f_x$Differentiate w.r.t $x$, treat $y$ as constant.
First-Order (w.r.t $y$)$f_y$Differentiate w.r.t $y$, treat $x$ as constant.
Lagrange Multiplier$f_\lambda$Differentiate w.r.t $\lambda$, treat $x,y$ as constants.
Second-Order Pure$f_{xx}$Take derivative of $f_x$ w.r.t $x$ again.
Second-Order Mixed$f_{xy}$Take derivative of $f_x$ w.r.t $y$.

Instructor Insight: Product & Quotient Rules Apply!

If you are differentiating $f(x,y) = \frac{xy}{5x+4y}$ with respect to $x$, the variable $x$ is in both the numerator and denominator. You must use the Quotient Rule, treating $y$ as a constant scalar.

Example: Mixed Partials & Chain/Product Rules

Problem: Given $f(x,y) = e^{10xy}$, calculate $f_x$, $f_{xx}$, and the mixed partial $f_{xy}$.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

First-Order ($f_x$): Chain rule. Treat $10y$ as a constant multiplier. $$ f_x = e^{10xy} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(10xy) = 10y e^{10xy} $$

Pure Second-Order ($f_{xx}$): $10y$ is still a constant scalar. $$ f_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(10y e^{10xy}) = 10y \cdot (10y e^{10xy}) = 100y^2 e^{10xy} $$

Mixed Partial ($f_{xy}$): Take the derivative of $f_x$ w.r.t $y$. Product Rule required since $y$ is in both terms! $$ f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y} [10y \cdot e^{10xy}] = [10] \cdot e^{10xy} + [10y] \cdot (10x e^{10xy}) = 10e^{10xy} (1 + 10xy) $$

Business Example: Cobb-Douglas Marginal Productivity

Problem: Given the production function $p(x,y) = 2100 x^{3/5} y^{2/5}$ (where $x$ is labor units, $y$ is capital), find the marginal productivities $p_x$ and $p_y$.

Apply Solution Standards: Draft justification before revealing.

Find $p_x$ (Hold $y$ constant): $$ p_x = 2100 y^{2/5} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[x^{3/5}] = 2100 y^{2/5} \cdot \left(\frac{3}{5}x^{-2/5}\right) = 1260 \frac{y^{2/5}}{x^{2/5}} $$

Find $p_y$ (Hold $x$ constant): $$ p_y = 2100 x^{3/5} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[y^{2/5}] = 2100 x^{3/5} \cdot \left(\frac{2}{5}y^{-3/5}\right) = 840 \frac{x^{3/5}}{y^{3/5}} $$

Interpretation: If you evaluate $p_x(31, 1422)$, it gives the approximate increase in total production generated by adding one unit of labor, strictly assuming capital is held fixed at 1422 units.
