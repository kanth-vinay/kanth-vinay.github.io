Chapters 5–7: Inner Products, Linear Transformations, and Eigenvalues

How to use this guide: This review breaks down complex, multi-part exam problems into fundamental skills. Do not read this passively. Cover the solution, attempt each item on your own, and then compare your reasoning with the worked explanation.
WebAssign Formatting Note: When entering matrices into WebAssign, you may need to use bracket and semicolon notation. 1. Vectors, Norms, and Inner Products (Ch 5)

Mastering basic vector operations and adapting to non-standard inner products.

Item 1: Magnitude of a Vector

Find the length (magnitude) of the vector \(v = (8, -15)\) under the standard Euclidean inner product.

Answer: 17

The length of a vector \(v = (v_1, v_2)\) is given by \(\|v\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}\).

\[ \|v\| = \sqrt{8^2 + (-15)^2} = \sqrt{64 + 225} = \sqrt{289} = 17 \]

Item 2: Opposite Unit Vector

Find a unit vector in the direction opposite to \(u = (-3, 4)\).

Answer: \(\left\langle \frac{3}{5}, -\frac{4}{5} \right\rangle\)

Step 1: Find the length of \(u\). \(\|u\| = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9+16} = 5\).

Step 2: A unit vector in the same direction is \(\frac{u}{\|u\|} = \left(-\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right)\).

Step 3: To find the opposite direction, multiply by \(-1\): \(\left(\frac{3}{5}, -\frac{4}{5}\right)\).

Exam Trap: Forgetting to distribute the negative sign to both components when asked for the "opposite" direction.
Item 3: Evaluating Custom Inner Products

Given the inner product \(\langle u,v \rangle = 2u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + 3u_3v_3\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), find \(\langle u,v \rangle\) for \(u = (1, -1, 2)\) and \(v = (0, 2, 1)\).

Answer: 4

Substitute the components of \(u\) and \(v\) into the given formula:

\[ \langle u,v \rangle = 2(1)(0) + (-1)(2) + 3(2)(1) \]

\[ \langle u,v \rangle = 0 - 2 + 6 = 4 \]

Item 4: Norm Under a Custom Inner Product

Using the same inner product \(\langle u,v \rangle = 2u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + 3u_3v_3\), find \(\|u\|\) for \(u = (2, 3, -1)\).

Answer: \(\sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\)

The norm is defined as \(\|u\| = \sqrt{\langle u,u \rangle}\). First, find \(\langle u,u \rangle\):

\[ \langle u,u \rangle = 2(2)(2) + (3)(3) + 3(-1)(-1) = 8 + 9 + 3 = 20 \]

Take the square root to find the norm: \(\sqrt{20}\).

Exam Trap: A very common mistake is finding \(\langle u,u \rangle\) and forgetting to take the square root at the very end to find the norm.
Item 5: Distance Under a Custom Inner Product

Using the inner product \(\langle u,v \rangle = 2u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + 3u_3v_3\), find the distance \(d(u,v)\) between \(u = (2, 1, 0)\) and \(v = (0, 2, -1)\).

Answer: \(2\sqrt{3}\)

The distance formula is \(d(u,v) = \|u - v\| = \sqrt{\langle u-v, u-v \rangle}\).

Step 1: Find the vector difference \(u - v\):
\((2, 1, 0) - (0, 2, -1) = (2, -1, 1)\).

Step 2: Apply the custom inner product to \((2, -1, 1)\) with itself:
\(\langle u-v, u-v \rangle = 2(2)(2) + (-1)(-1) + 3(1)(1) = 8 + 1 + 3 = 12\).

Step 3: Take the square root: \(\sqrt{12} = 2\sqrt{3}\).

2. Orthogonality & Gram-Schmidt (Ch 5)

Constructing orthogonal bases requires meticulous step-by-step arithmetic.

Item 6: Checking Orthogonality

Show whether the set of vectors \(\{(-2, 4), (6, 3)\}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is orthogonal.

Answer: Yes, they are orthogonal.

Two vectors are orthogonal if their inner (dot) product is zero.

\[ \langle (-2, 4), (6, 3) \rangle = (-2)(6) + (4)(3) = -12 + 12 = 0 \]

Since the dot product is exactly \(0\), the vectors are orthogonal.

Item 7: Normalizing an Orthogonal Set

Normalize the orthogonal set \(\{(-2, 4), (6, 3)\}\) to produce an orthonormal set.

Answer: \(\left\{ \left\langle -\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}, \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} \right\rangle, \left\langle \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \right\rangle \right\}\)

Divide each vector by its own magnitude.

For \(v_1 = (-2, 4)\), \(\|v_1\| = \sqrt{4 + 16} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\).
Normalized \(u_1 = \left(-\frac{2}{2\sqrt{5}}, \frac{4}{2\sqrt{5}}\right) = \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}, \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}\right)\).

For \(v_2 = (6, 3)\), \(\|v_2\| = \sqrt{36 + 9} = \sqrt{45} = 3\sqrt{5}\).
Normalized \(u_2 = \left(\frac{6}{3\sqrt{5}}, \frac{3}{3\sqrt{5}}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\right)\).

Item 8: Gram-Schmidt Step 1 (First Vector)

Apply the Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization process to the basis \(B = \{(3, 4), (0, 5)\}\). What is the first vector \(u_1\) in the orthonormal basis?

Answer: \(\left\langle \frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5} \right\rangle\)

The Gram-Schmidt process begins by taking the first vector \(v_1 = (3,4)\) and normalizing it.

\[ \|v_1\| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5 \]

\[ u_1 = \frac{v_1}{\|v_1\|} = \left(\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right) \]

Item 9: Gram-Schmidt Step 2 (Second Vector)

Continuing from Item 8 with \(v_2 = (0, 5)\) and \(u_1 = \left(\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right)\), calculate the second orthogonal vector \(w_2\), and then normalize it to find \(u_2\).

Answer: \(u_2 = \left\langle -\frac{4}{5}, \frac{3}{5} \right\rangle\)

Step 1 (Find orthogonal \(w_2\)): \(w_2 = v_2 - \langle v_2, u_1 \rangle u_1\)

First, calculate the projection coefficient: \(\langle (0,5), \left(\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right) \rangle = 0 + \frac{20}{5} = 4\).

Subtract the projection from \(v_2\):
\(w_2 = (0, 5) - 4\left(\frac{3}{5}, \frac{4}{5}\right) = (0, 5) - \left(\frac{12}{5}, \frac{16}{5}\right) = \left(-\frac{12}{5}, \frac{9}{5}\right)\).

Step 2 (Normalize \(w_2\)):
\(\|w_2\| = \sqrt{\left(-\frac{12}{5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{9}{5}\right)^2} = \sqrt{\frac{144 + 81}{25}} = \sqrt{\frac{225}{25}} = \frac{15}{5} = 3\).

\[ u_2 = \frac{w_2}{\|w_2\|} = \frac{1}{3} \left(-\frac{12}{5}, \frac{9}{5}\right) = \left(-\frac{4}{5}, \frac{3}{5}\right) \]

Exam Trap: Do not mix the two projection formulas. If you project onto a normalized vector, use \(\langle v_2,u_1\rangle u_1\). If you project onto a non-unit vector, use \(\frac{\langle v_2,w_1\rangle}{\langle w_1,w_1\rangle}w_1\).

3. Least Squares Regression (Ch 5)

Setting up and solving the normal equations \(A^T A x = A^T b\).

Item 10: Setting up the Matrices

Set up the matrix \(A\) and vector \(b\) to find the least squares regression line \(y = c_0 + c_1x\) for the data points: \((-1, 1), (0, 1), (1, 4)\).

Answer: \(A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}\), \(b = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1 \\ 4\end{bmatrix}\)

The equation of the line is \(c_0(1) + c_1(x) = y\). Create a system of equations by plugging in the points:

Point \((-1,1)\): \(c_0 + c_1(-1) = 1\)

Point \((0,1)\): \(c_0 + c_1(0) = 1\)

Point \((1,4)\): \(c_0 + c_1(1) = 4\)

Extract the coefficients into matrix \(A\) (the first column is always 1s for the \(c_0\) intercept term) and the \(y\)-values into vector \(b\).

Item 11: Solving the Normal Equations

Using the \(A\) and \(b\) from Item 10, compute \(A^T A\) and \(A^T b\), then solve for the line \(y = c_0 + c_1x\).

Answer: \(y = 2 + 1.5x\)

Step 1: Compute \(A^T A\)

\[ A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Compute \(A^T b\)

\[ A^T b = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1 \\ 4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6 \\ 3\end{bmatrix} \]

Step 3: Solve \(A^T A x = A^T b\)

\[ \begin{bmatrix}3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}c_0 \\ c_1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6 \\ 3\end{bmatrix} \]

This gives \(3c_0 = 6 \implies c_0 = 2\), and \(2c_1 = 3 \implies c_1 = 1.5\). The line is \(y = 2 + 1.5x\).

Item 12: Selecting the Correct Least Squares Graph

After calculating a regression line, how do you choose the correct graph from multiple options if the data points trend downward?

Answer: Verify the data points match exactly, and ensure the line has a negative slope.

Step 1 (Check Points): Look strictly at the plotted dots. Discard any graph where the dots do not perfectly match your given data coordinates.

Step 2 (Check Slope): If the data trend downward (from top-left to bottom-right), the computed slope \(c_1\) must be negative, and the selected line must point downward.

Step 3 (Check y-intercept): Ensure the line crosses the y-axis at the computed \(c_0\) intercept.

4. Linear Transformations and Properties (Ch 6)

Working with images, preimages, the kernel, rank, and nullity.

Item 13: Finding the Image

Let \(T(v_1, v_2) = (v_1 + 2v_2, v_1 - v_2)\). Find the image of \(v = (4, -1)\).

Answer: \((2, 5)\)

Simply plug \(v_1 = 4\) and \(v_2 = -1\) into the transformation rule:

\[ T(4, -1) = (4 + 2(-1), 4 - (-1)) = (2, 5) \]

Item 14: Finding a Unique Preimage

Using \(T(v_1, v_2) = (v_1 + 2v_2, v_1 - v_2)\), find the preimage of \(w = (7, 1)\).

Answer: \((3, 2)\)

Set the transformation equal to the target vector to create a system of equations:

\(v_1 + 2v_2 = 7\)
\(v_1 - v_2 = 1\)

Subtract the second equation from the first: \(3v_2 = 6 \implies v_2 = 2\).

Substitute back: \(v_1 - 2 = 1 \implies v_1 = 3\). The preimage is \((3, 2)\).

Item 15: Finding a Preimage (Infinite Solutions)

Let \(T(x, y, z) = (x + y, y + z)\). Find the preimage of \(w = (5, 8)\) in terms of the parameter \(t\).

Answer: \((5-t, t, 8-t)\)

Set up the equations: \(x + y = 5\) and \(y + z = 8\).

Since we have 3 variables and 2 equations, there are infinite solutions. Let \(y = t\).

From the first equation: \(x + t = 5 \implies x = 5 - t\).

From the second equation: \(t + z = 8 \implies z = 8 - t\).

The full vector is \((5-t, t, 8-t)\).

Item 16: Matrix Dimensions and Mapping

A linear transformation \(T(v) = Av\) uses a matrix \(A\) of size \(3 \times 5\). Find the dimensions of the domain (\(\mathbb{R}^n\)) and codomain (\(\mathbb{R}^m\)).

Answer: Domain: \(\mathbb{R}^5\), Codomain: \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

An \(m \times n\) matrix transforms a vector from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) (columns) to \(\mathbb{R}^m\) (rows).

Since the matrix is \(3 \times 5\), \(n=5\) (dimension of domain) and \(m=3\) (dimension of codomain).

Exam Trap: It's easy to flip these. Remember: Matrix multiplication \(A_{m \times n} \cdot v_{n \times 1} = w_{m \times 1}\). The input vector size must match the number of columns of \(A\).
Item 17: Rank-Nullity Computation

Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^4 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) be a linear transformation. If \(\operatorname{rank}(T) = 2\), find the nullity of \(T\).

Answer: 2

The Rank-Nullity Theorem states: \(\operatorname{rank}(T) + \operatorname{nullity}(T) = n\) (the dimension of the domain).

Here, the domain is \(\mathbb{R}^4\), so \(n = 4\).

\(2 + \operatorname{nullity}(T) = 4 \implies \operatorname{nullity}(T) = 2\).

Item 18: Geometry of Kernel and Range

For a transformation \(T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3\), if \(\operatorname{rank}(T) = 2\), give a geometric description of the kernel and the range.

Answer: Kernel is a line; Range is a plane.

By Rank-Nullity, \(2 + \operatorname{nullity}(T) = 3 \implies \operatorname{nullity}(T) = 1\).

The kernel is a subspace of the domain (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) with dimension 1, which represents a line through the origin.

The range is a subspace of the codomain (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)) with dimension 2 (the rank), which represents a plane through the origin.

Item 19: Checking One-to-One and Onto (Projection)

Determine if \(T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) defined by \(T(x,y,z) = (x, y, 0)\) is one-to-one, onto, neither, or both.

Answer: Neither

One-to-One: A transformation is 1-1 if its kernel contains only the zero vector. Notice that \(T(0,0,5) = (0,0,0)\). Since a non-zero vector maps to zero, it is not 1-1.

Onto: A transformation is onto if its range is the entire codomain. The range here only produces vectors with 0 in the z-component (the xy-plane). It cannot reach a vector like \((1,1,1)\), so it is not onto.

Item 20: Coordinate Permutation (One-to-One & Onto)

Determine if \(T: \mathbb{R}^4 \to \mathbb{R}^4\) defined by \(T(x,y,z,w) = (w,z,x,y)\) is one-to-one, onto, neither, or both.

Answer: Both (One-to-One and Onto)

This transformation simply rearranges the input coordinates without collapsing or losing any information.

One-to-One: The only way to get \((0,0,0,0)\) as an output is if you put \((0,0,0,0)\) in as the input. Since the kernel is only the zero vector, it is 1-1.

Onto: Every possible vector \((a,b,c,d)\) in the codomain can be reached. For example, to output \((a,b,c,d)\), you simply input its exact preimage: \((c,d,b,a)\). Because its range covers the entire codomain, it is onto.

5. Standard Matrices and Inverses (Ch 6)

Converting transformations into matrix representations and finding inverses.

Item 21: Finding the Standard Matrix

Find the standard matrix \(A\) for the linear transformation \(T(x,y) = (-2x + 3y, x - y)\).

Answer: \(A = \begin{bmatrix}-2 & 3 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}\)

To find the standard matrix, evaluate \(T\) on the standard basis vectors \(e_1 = (1,0)\) and \(e_2 = (0,1)\).

\(T(1,0) = (-2(1) + 3(0), 1 - 0) = (-2, 1)\). This is the first column.

\(T(0,1) = (-2(0) + 3(1), 0 - 1) = (3, -1)\). This is the second column.

Item 22: Inverse of a 2x2 Matrix

Find the inverse of \(A = \begin{bmatrix}-2 & 3 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix}1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\)

For a 2x2 matrix \(\begin{bmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{bmatrix}\), the inverse is \(\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{bmatrix}\).

Determinant: \((-2)(-1) - (3)(1) = 2 - 3 = -1\).

Swap \(a\) and \(d\), negate \(b\) and \(c\), then divide by \(-1\):

\[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-1} \begin{bmatrix}-1 & -3 \\ -1 & -2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} \]

Item 23: The Inverse Transformation

Using the inverse matrix from Item 22, define the inverse linear transformation \(T^{-1}(x,y)\).

Answer: \(T^{-1}(x,y) = (x + 3y, x + 2y)\)

Multiply the inverse matrix by the column vector \(\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix}\):

\[ \begin{bmatrix}1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1x + 3y \\ 1x + 2y\end{bmatrix} \]

Write it as a transformation: \((x + 3y, x + 2y)\).

6. Eigenvalues, Eigenvectors, and Diagonalization (Ch 7)

Finding roots of the characteristic equation and constructing the diagonalizing matrix P.

Item 24: Eigenvalues of a Triangular Matrix

Find the eigenvalues of the upper triangular matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & -2 & 5 \\ 0 & -3 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \(\lambda = -3, 2, 7\)

For any triangular or diagonal matrix, the eigenvalues are simply the entries on the main diagonal.

The diagonal entries are 7, -3, and 2. WebAssign often asks for them from smallest to largest.

Item 25: 3x3 Characteristic Equation & Eigenvalues

Find the characteristic equation and the eigenvalues of the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & -1 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \((\lambda-1)(\lambda-2)(\lambda-3) = 0\); Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = 1, 2, 3\)

Set \(\det(\lambda I - A) = 0\):

\[ \det \begin{bmatrix} \lambda - 1 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & \lambda - 4 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 & \lambda - 1 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \]

Expand along the first column (since it has two zeros):

\[ (\lambda - 1) \det \begin{bmatrix} \lambda - 4 & 1 \\ -2 & \lambda - 1 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \]

\[ (\lambda - 1) [(\lambda - 4)(\lambda - 1) - (1)(-2)] = 0 \]

\[ (\lambda - 1) [\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 4 + 2] = 0 \]

\[ (\lambda - 1) (\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6) = 0 \]

Factor the quadratic to get the characteristic equation: \((\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 3) = 0\). The eigenvalues are the roots: 1, 2, and 3.

Item 26: Finding 3x3 Eigenspaces

Using the matrix \(A\) from Item 25 and its eigenvalues \(\lambda = 1, 2, 3\), find a basis for each corresponding eigenspace.

Answer: For \(\lambda=1\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\); For \(\lambda=2\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\); For \(\lambda=3\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\)

Solve \((\lambda I - A)x = 0\) for each eigenvalue.

For \(\lambda = 1\): Substitute 1 into \(\lambda I - A\) to get \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & -3 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\). From row 3, \(-2y = 0 \implies y=0\). From row 2, \(-3y + z = 0 \implies z=0\). \(x\) is a free variable. Letting \(x=1\) gives \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

For \(\lambda = 2\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\). Row 2 implies \(z = 2y\). Substitute into row 1: \(x - 2y - 3(2y) = 0 \implies x = 8y\). Letting \(y=1\) gives \(z=2\) and \(x=8\), resulting in \(\begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

For \(\lambda = 3\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\). Row 2 implies \(z = y\). Substitute into row 1: \(2x - 2y - 3(y) = 0 \implies 2x = 5y\). To avoid fractions, let \(y=2\). Then \(z=2\) and \(x=5\), resulting in \(\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

Item 27: Finding the Characteristic Equation (2x2)

Find the characteristic equation of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \(\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 = 0\)

Set \(\det(\lambda I - A) = 0\):

\[ \det \begin{bmatrix} \lambda - 5 & -2 \\ 1 & \lambda - 2 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \]

\[ (\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) - (-2)(1) = 0 \]

\[ \lambda^2 - 2\lambda - 5\lambda + 10 + 2 = 0 \]

\[ \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 = 0 \]

Item 28: Finding Roots (Eigenvalues 2x2)

Factor the characteristic equation \(\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 = 0\) to find the eigenvalues of the matrix from Item 27.

Answer: \(\lambda_1 = 3, \lambda_2 = 4\)

Factor the quadratic: \((\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 4) = 0\).

Setting each factor to zero yields the eigenvalues \(\lambda = 3\) and \(\lambda = 4\).

Item 29: Finding a Basis for the Eigenspace (2x2)

Find a basis for the eigenspace corresponding to \(\lambda = 4\) for the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

Substitute \(\lambda = 4\) into the system \((\lambda I - A)x = 0\):

\[ 4I - A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 - 5 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 - 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \]

This gives the equation \(-x - 2y = 0 \implies x = -2y\).

If we let \(y = 1\), then \(x = -2\). The basis vector is \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Exam Trap: Eigenspaces are spans. Any non-zero scalar multiple of \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\), such as \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\), is mathematically correct, though systems like WebAssign usually prefer the simplest integers.
Item 30: Geometric Method for Simple Eigenspaces

Use geometric intuition to find the eigenvalues and eigenspaces of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -4 & 0 \\ 0 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\).

Answer: \(\lambda_1 = -4\), span([1; 0]); \(\lambda_2 = 8\), span([0; 1])

This is a diagonal matrix mapping the x-axis to -4 times itself, and the y-axis to 8 times itself.

The eigenvalues are the diagonal entries: -4 and 8.

The eigenspace for -4 is the entire x-axis, spanned by \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).

The eigenspace for 8 is the entire y-axis, spanned by \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Item 31: Building the Diagonalizing Matrix P

Suppose matrix \(A\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 3\) and \(\lambda_2 = 4\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(p_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(p_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\). Form the matrix \(P\) that diagonalizes \(A\).

Answer: \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

The matrix \(P\) is formed by using the eigenvectors as columns.

\[ P = \begin{bmatrix} | & | \\ p_1 & p_2 \\ | & | \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Item 32: Structure of the Diagonal Matrix D

Without computing the full matrix multiplication \(P^{-1}AP\), what will be the resulting matrix \(D\) for the setup in Item 31?

Answer: \(D = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(P^{-1}AP\) always produces a diagonal matrix \(D\) where the entries on the main diagonal are the eigenvalues of \(A\).

Crucial Rule: The order of the eigenvalues in \(D\) must match the order of the corresponding eigenvectors placed as columns in \(P\).

Since we placed the eigenvector for \(\lambda_1 = 3\) in the first column of \(P\), \(3\) must be the first entry in \(D\).

Item 33: Full Diagonalization Procedure (2x2)

Find a nonsingular matrix \(P\) such that \(P^{-1}AP\) is diagonal for \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\), and state the diagonal matrix \(D\).

Answer: \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\), \(D = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\)

Step 1 (Eigenvalues): Solve \(\det(\lambda I - A) = 0 \implies (\lambda-6)(\lambda-1) - (-2)(-3) = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda = 0\). The eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 0\) and \(\lambda_2 = 7\).

Step 2 (Eigenvectors):
For \(\lambda_1 = 0\): \(0I - A = \begin{bmatrix} -6 & 3 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \implies -6x + 3y = 0 \implies y = 2x\). Let \(x=1\), so \(p_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\).
For \(\lambda_2 = 7\): \(7I - A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \implies x + 3y = 0 \implies x = -3y\). Let \(y=1\), so \(p_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Step 3 (Assemble Matrices):
Place the eigenvectors as columns to form \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).
Place the corresponding eigenvalues on the main diagonal to form \(D = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\).

Exam Trap: The order matters! Because you placed the eigenvector for \(\lambda=7\) in the second column of \(P\), \(7\) must be the second entry on the diagonal of \(D\).
Item 34: Complex Characteristic Equations Trap

Given the characteristic equation \((\lambda-3)(\lambda^2 + 1) = 0\), how many real eigenvalues does the matrix have?

Answer: Only one (\(\lambda = 3\)).

Solving the characteristic equation yields \(\lambda - 3 = 0 \implies \lambda = 3\).

The second factor gives \(\lambda^2 + 1 = 0 \implies \lambda^2 = -1 \implies \lambda = \pm i\).

Because \(\pm i\) are not real numbers, they are not considered real eigenvalues, and a matrix with this characteristic equation is not diagonalizable over the real numbers.
