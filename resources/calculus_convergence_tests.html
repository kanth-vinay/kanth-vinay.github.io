Foundations: Sequences, Series, and Partial Sums

Sequence vs. Series: A sequence is an ordered list of terms: $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. A series is the infinite sum of those terms: $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n = a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \dots$.
Partial Sums: Since we cannot physically add infinite numbers, we look at the $n$-th partial sum: $S_n = a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n$.
The Core Principle: A series converges if and only if the sequence of its partial sums converges to a finite limit. If $\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n = S$, then the series converges to $S$.

Critical AP Warning

The limit of the terms going to zero ($\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0$) does NOT prove convergence. It is only a necessary condition, not a sufficient one. The classic counterexample is the harmonic series $\sum \frac{1}{n}$. The terms go to zero, but the series diverges.

1. The n-th Term Test for Divergence

Standard Theorem

If the sequence of terms does not approach zero, the series cannot converge.

If $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0$, then $\sum a_n$ diverges.

When to think of this test:

Almost always check this mentally first! Especially useful when the numerator and denominator have the same degree, or if it involves oscillating terms like $\cos(n\pi)$.

When NOT to use it:

When the limit is $0$. If $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0$, the test is inconclusive. You must find another tool.

Checklist & Strategy

  1. Identify the expression for $a_n$.
  2. Take the limit as $n \to \infty$.
  3. If it equals a non-zero number, state "Diverges by n-th term test."
  4. If it equals exactly 0, move on to a different test immediately.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n}{n+1}$

Reasoning: Degrees are equal, horizontal asymptote check.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n}{n+1} = 1 \neq 0$
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
2. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{3n+1}{2n-5}$

Reasoning: Highest power of $n$ in numerator and denominator is $n^1$.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3n+1}{2n-5} = \frac{3}{2} \neq 0$
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
3. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \cos(n\pi)$

Reasoning: The terms $\cos(n\pi)$ alternate between $-1$ and $1$.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \cos(n\pi)$ DNE (oscillates). Limit is not zero.
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
4. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sin(n)$

Reasoning: $\sin(n)$ takes pseudo-random values in $[-1, 1]$.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \sin(n)$ DNE. Terms do not approach zero.
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
5. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n^2+1}{5n^2-3}$

Reasoning: Rational expression, degrees are equal ($n^2$).

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^2+1}{5n^2-3} = \frac{1}{5} \neq 0$
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
6. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}$

Reasoning: Classic harmonic series.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0$
Conclusion: Test Inconclusive
7. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$

Reasoning: Degree in denominator is higher.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = 0$
Conclusion: Test Inconclusive
8. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\ln n}{n}$

Reasoning: L'Hôpital's rule shows $n$ grows faster than $\ln n$.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\ln n}{n} = 0$
Conclusion: Test Inconclusive
9. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$

Reasoning: Denominator grows to infinity.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = 0$
Conclusion: Test Inconclusive
10. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1+(-1)^n}{n}$

Reasoning: Squeeze theorem: terms are bounded between $0$ and $2/n$.

$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1+(-1)^n}{n} = 0$
Conclusion: Test Inconclusive

2. Geometric Series

Standard Theorem

A geometric series $\sum ar^n$ has a constant ratio $r$ between successive terms.

Converges if $|r| < 1$. Diverges if $|r| \ge 1$.
If it converges, its sum is $S = \frac{a_1}{1 - r}$, where $a_1$ is the first term.

When to think of it:

When you see an index $n$ in the exponent (like $2^n$ or $(3/4)^{n-1}$).

Common Mistake

Getting the first term wrong. Always plug in the starting index (e.g., $n=0$ or $n=1$) to find $a_1$ before using the sum formula.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (1/2)^n$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = 1/2$. $|1/2| < 1$. First term $a = (1/2)^0 = 1$.

Sum = $\frac{1}{1 - 1/2} = 2$
Conclusion: Converges
2. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 3(2/3)^{n-1}$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = 2/3$. $|2/3| < 1$. First term (plug in $n=1$) $a = 3(2/3)^0 = 3$.

Sum = $\frac{3}{1 - 2/3} = 9$
Conclusion: Converges
3. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1/3)^n$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = -1/3$. $|-1/3| < 1$. First term $a = 1$.

Sum = $\frac{1}{1 - (-1/3)} = \frac{3}{4}$
Conclusion: Converges absolutely
4. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} 5(1/4)^n$

Reasoning: Missing the first two terms! First term is $5(1/4)^2 = 5/16$. $r=1/4$.

Sum = $\frac{5/16}{1 - 1/4} = \frac{5}{12}$
Conclusion: Converges
5. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 7(-1/2)^n$

Reasoning: $r = -1/2$. First term $a = 7(-1/2)^1 = -7/2$.

Sum = $\frac{-7/2}{1 - (-1/2)} = -\frac{7}{3}$
Conclusion: Converges absolutely
6. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 2^n$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = 2$. $|2| \ge 1$.

Conclusion: Diverges
7. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^n$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = -1$. $|-1| \ge 1$. The sum oscillates.

Conclusion: Diverges
8. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (5/4)^n$

Reasoning: Ratio $r = 5/4 = 1.25$. $|5/4| \ge 1$.

Conclusion: Diverges
9. $\sum_{n=3}^{\infty} 6(1/5)^{n-3}$

Reasoning: Index shift. First term (plug in $n=3$) is $6(1/5)^0 = 6$. $r=1/5$.

Sum = $\frac{6}{1 - 1/5} = \frac{30}{4} = \frac{15}{2}$
Conclusion: Converges
10. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} -4(3/10)^n$

Reasoning: Geometric with $r = 3/10$. First term $a = -4$.

Sum = $\frac{-4}{1 - 3/10} = -\frac{40}{7}$
Conclusion: Converges

3. The p-Series Test

Standard Theorem

The series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p}$ converges if $p > 1$, and diverges if $p \le 1$.

When to think of it:

When you have $n$ raised to a constant power (not a constant raised to the $n$th power).

Common Mistake

Mixing up $p>1$ with Ratio Test's $L<1$. For $p$-series, bigger powers in the denominator mean terms shrink faster, causing CONVERGENCE when $p>1$.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$

Reasoning: Identified as a p-series. $p=2$.

Since $2 > 1$, it converges.
Conclusion: Converges
2. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{3/2}}$

Reasoning: Identified as a p-series. $p=3/2 = 1.5$.

Since $1.5 > 1$, it converges.
Conclusion: Converges
3. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{1.01}}$

Reasoning: Identified as a p-series. $p=1.01$.

Barely, but since $1.01 > 1$, it converges.
Conclusion: Converges
4. $\sum \frac{1}{n}$

Reasoning: The Harmonic series. Equivalent to p-series with $p=1$.

Since $1 \le 1$, it diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
5. $\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$

Reasoning: Can be rewritten as $\sum 1/n^{1/2}$. $p=1/2$.

Since $1/2 \le 1$, it diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
6. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{0.9}}$

Reasoning: Identified as a p-series. $p=0.9$.

Since $0.9 \le 1$, it diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
7. $\sum \frac{5}{n^4}$

Reasoning: Constant multiple of a p-series where $p=4$.

Since $4 > 1$, the underlying series converges, so $5 \times$ convergent is convergent.
Conclusion: Converges
8. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{7/3}}$

Reasoning: $p=7/3 \approx 2.33$.

Since $7/3 > 1$, it converges.
Conclusion: Converges
9. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{2/3}}$

Reasoning: $p=2/3 \approx 0.67$.

Since $2/3 \le 1$, it diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
10. $\sum \frac{1}{(n\pi)^2}$

Reasoning: Expand to $\frac{1}{\pi^2} \sum \frac{1}{n^2}$. This is a constant multiple of a $p$-series where $p=2$.

Since $2 > 1$, it converges.
Conclusion: Converges

4. The Integral Test

Standard Theorem

Let $f(x)$ be continuous, positive, and decreasing for $x \ge 1$, and let $a_n = f(n)$.

$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ and $\int_{1}^{\infty} f(x) dx$ either BOTH converge or BOTH diverge.

When to think of it:

When you look at the $n$-th term and immediately think "I know the antiderivative for that." Classical markers: $n$ and $\ln(n)$ pairs.

Common Mistake

Forgetting to verify the 3 conditions: Continuous, Positive, Decreasing on the integration interval. A loose statement costs points on AP FRQs.

Worked Examples

Note: For all examples below, you must explicitly state that $f(x)$ is continuous, positive, and decreasing on the appropriate interval.

1. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x(\ln x)^2}$. For $x \ge 2$, $f$ is continuous, positive, and decreasing. Evaluate integral using $u$-substitution $u = \ln x, du = \frac{1}{x}dx$.

$\int_2^\infty u^{-2} du = \lim_{b\to\infty} \left[ -\frac{1}{\ln x} \right]_2^b = 0 - \left( -\frac{1}{\ln 2} \right) = \frac{1}{\ln 2}$

The improper integral converges.

Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
2. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln n}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln x}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing for $x \ge 2$. Substitution $u = \ln x$.

$\int_2^\infty \frac{du}{u} = \lim_{b\to\infty} [\ln |\ln x|]_2^b = \infty$

The improper integral diverges.

Conclusion: Diverges (by Integral Test)
3. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+1}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2+1}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing for $x \ge 1$. Classic inverse tangent integral.

$\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^2+1} dx = \lim_{b\to\infty} [\arctan(x)]_1^b = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{4}$
Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
4. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+4}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2+4}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing. Use arctan integral rules.

$\lim_{b\to\infty} \left[\frac{1}{2} \arctan \left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\right]_1^b = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \arctan(1/2)\right)$
Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
5. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^3}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x(\ln x)^3}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing for $x \ge 2$. $u = \ln x$.

$\int u^{-3} du = \lim_{b\to\infty} \left[ -\frac{1}{2(\ln x)^2} \right]_2^b = \frac{1}{2(\ln 2)^2}$
Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
6. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\ln n}{n^2}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{\ln x}{x^2}$. Continuous, positive for $x \ge 2$. Decreasing eventually (derive $f'(x) = \frac{1-2\ln x}{x^3} < 0$ for $x > \sqrt{e}$). Use integration by parts.

$\int_2^\infty x^{-2} \ln(x) dx$. Eval yields finite bound.
Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
7. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2+1}}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing. Integral evaluates to $\ln|x+\sqrt{x^2+1}|$.

$\lim_{b\to\infty} \ln|b+\sqrt{b^2+1}| - \ln(1+\sqrt{2}) = \infty$
Conclusion: Diverges (by Integral Test)
8. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n+1}$

Let $f(x) = \frac{1}{x+1}$. Continuous, positive, decreasing.

$\int_1^\infty \frac{dx}{x+1} = \lim_{b\to\infty} [\ln(x+1)]_1^b = \infty$
Conclusion: Diverges (by Integral Test)
9. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n(\ln n)(\ln(\ln n))^2}$

Let bounds begin at $n=3$ to keep logs positive. Let $u = \ln(\ln x)$.

$\int u^{-2} du = [-\frac{1}{u}] \to \text{Finite value.}$
Conclusion: Converges (by Integral Test)
10. $\sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln n \ln(\ln n)}$

Let $u = \ln(\ln x)$. Then $du = \frac{1}{x \ln x} dx$.

$\int \frac{1}{u} du = [\ln|\ln(\ln x)|]_3^\infty = \infty$
Conclusion: Diverges (by Integral Test)

5. The Direct Comparison Test (DCT)

Standard Theorem

Let $0 \le a_n \le b_n$ for all $n$ (or at least for $n \ge M$).

If the "larger" series $\sum b_n$ converges, the "smaller" series $\sum a_n$ converges.
If the "smaller" series $\sum a_n$ diverges, the "larger" series $\sum b_n$ diverges.

When to think of it:

When the term looks almost exactly like a known p-series or geometric series, but has a $+1$ or $-\cos(n)$ that makes it strictly smaller or larger.

Common Mistake

Getting the inequality backward. Knowing that $a_n < b_n$ and $\sum b_n$ diverges tells you absolutely nothing about $\sum a_n$. The inequalities must go in the correct direction!

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \frac{1}{n^2+1}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{1}{n^2}$. Since $n^2+1 > n^2 \implies \frac{1}{n^2+1} < \frac{1}{n^2}$.

$\sum 1/n^2$ converges (p=2>1). Smaller than convergent.
Conclusion: Converges
2. $\sum \frac{1}{n^2+n}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{1}{n^2}$. $n^2+n > n^2 \implies \frac{1}{n^2+n} < \frac{1}{n^2}$.

$\sum 1/n^2$ converges (p=2>1). Smaller than convergent.
Conclusion: Converges
3. $\sum \frac{1}{n^3+5}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{1}{n^3}$. $n^3+5 > n^3 \implies \frac{1}{n^3+5} < \frac{1}{n^3}$.

$\sum 1/n^3$ converges (p=3>1). Smaller than convergent.
Conclusion: Converges
4. $\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+1}$

Reasoning: Comparing to $1/\sqrt{n}$ directly gives $<$, which is useless for a divergent series. Instead, for $n \ge 1, \sqrt{n}+1 \le 2\sqrt{n}$, so $\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+1} \ge \frac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}$.

$\sum \frac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}$ diverges. Larger than divergent.
Conclusion: Diverges
5. $\sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n-2}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{1}{n}$. $n-2 < n \implies \frac{1}{n-2} > \frac{1}{n}$.

$\sum 1/n$ diverges. Larger than divergent.
Conclusion: Diverges
6. $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2-n}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{2}{n^2}$. For $n \ge 2$, $n^2/2 \ge n$, so $n^2-n \ge n^2/2 \implies \frac{1}{n^2-n} \le \frac{2}{n^2}$.

$\sum \frac{2}{n^2}$ converges. Smaller than convergent.
Conclusion: Converges
7. $\sum \frac{n}{n^3+1}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{n}{n^3} = \frac{1}{n^2}$. $\frac{n}{n^3+1} < \frac{n}{n^3} = \frac{1}{n^2}$.

Smaller than a convergent p-series.
Conclusion: Converges
8. $\sum \frac{n+1}{n^2}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{n}{n^2} = \frac{1}{n}$. $\frac{n+1}{n^2} > \frac{n}{n^2} = \frac{1}{n}$.

Larger than a divergent divergent harmonic series.
Conclusion: Diverges
9. $\sum \frac{1}{n^4+n}$

Reasoning: Compare to $\frac{1}{n^4}$. $n^4+n > n^4 \implies \frac{1}{n^4+n} < \frac{1}{n^4}$.

Smaller than a convergent p-series ($p=4$).
Conclusion: Converges
10. $\sum \frac{1}{n^{1/3}+5}$

Reasoning: For large $n$, $\frac{1}{n^{1/3}+5} \ge \frac{1}{2n^{1/3}}$.

Larger than divergent p-series ($p=1/3$).
Conclusion: Diverges

6. The Limit Comparison Test (LCT)

Standard Theorem

Let $a_n > 0$ and $b_n > 0$. If $\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = L$ where $0 < L < \infty$:

Both series converge, or both series diverge. (They share the same behavior.)

When to think of it:

When terms are ratios of polynomials or roots. Just grab the highest power in the numerator divided by the highest power in the denominator to choose your comparison series $b_n$.

Common Mistake

"Looks like" is not a theorem! On free-response questions, you must state the test, specify $b_n$, explicitly take the limit of the ratio, show $0 < L < \infty$, and then conclude.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \frac{2n+1}{n^2}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $2n/n^2 = 2/n$. Let $b_n = 1/n$.

$\lim \frac{(2n+1)/n^2}{1/n} = 2$. $0 < 2 < \infty$. $\sum 1/n$ diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
2. $\sum \frac{1}{n^2-n}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $1/n^2$. Let $b_n = 1/n^2$.

$\lim \frac{1/(n^2-n)}{1/n^2} = 1$. $0 < 1 < \infty$. $\sum 1/n^2$ converges.
Conclusion: Converges
3. $\sum \frac{3n^2+1}{n^3+2}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $3n^2/n^3 = 3/n$. Let $b_n = 1/n$.

$\lim \frac{(3n^2+1)/(n^3+2)}{1/n} = 3$. $0 < 3 < \infty$. $\sum 1/n$ diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
4. $\sum \frac{n+4}{n^2+7}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $n/n^2 = 1/n$. Let $b_n = 1/n$.

$\lim \frac{(n+4)/(n^2+7)}{1/n} = 1$. $\sum 1/n$ diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
5. $\sum \frac{5n^3-1}{2n^5+3}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $5n^3/2n^5 = (5/2)(1/n^2)$. Let $b_n = 1/n^2$.

$\lim \frac{(5n^3-1)/(2n^5+3)}{1/n^2} = 5/2$. $\sum 1/n^2$ converges.
Conclusion: Converges
6. $\sum \frac{\sqrt{n^2+1}}{n^3+1}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $\sqrt{n^2}/n^3 = n/n^3 = 1/n^2$. Let $b_n = 1/n^2$.

$\lim \frac{\sqrt{n^2+1}/(n^3+1)}{1/n^2} = 1$. $\sum 1/n^2$ converges.
Conclusion: Converges
7. $\sum \frac{n^2+5}{n^4+1}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $n^2/n^4 = 1/n^2$. Let $b_n = 1/n^2$.

$\lim \frac{(n^2+5)/(n^4+1)}{1/n^2} = 1$. $\sum 1/n^2$ converges.
Conclusion: Converges
8. $\sum \frac{4n-1}{n^2+9}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $4n/n^2 = 4/n$. Let $b_n = 1/n$.

$\lim \frac{(4n-1)/(n^2+9)}{1/n} = 4$. $\sum 1/n$ diverges.
Conclusion: Diverges
9. $\sum \frac{\ln n}{n}$ compared with $\frac{1}{n}$

CAUTION: DO NOT USE LCT HERE.

$\lim \frac{(\ln n)/n}{1/n} = \lim \ln n = \infty$
Because $L = \infty$, standard LCT fails. Use Integral Test or Direct Comparison (since $\ln n > 1$ for $n \ge 3$) instead.
10. $\sum \frac{n^2+\sin n}{3n^2+1}$

Reasoning: Behaves like $n^2/3n^2 = 1/3$. Actually, just use n-th term test!

LCT with $b_n=1$ yields $1/3$. $\sum 1$ diverges. Or simply note $a_n \to 1/3 \neq 0$.
Conclusion: Diverges

7. The Alternating Series Test (AST)

Standard Theorem

Let $\sum (-1)^{n} b_n$ be an alternating series where $b_n \ge 0$. The series converges if:

1) $b_{n+1} \le b_n$ eventually (terms are decreasing in magnitude)
2) $\lim_{n \to \infty} b_n = 0$

Checklist

  • Identify the positive portion $b_n$.
  • Show that terms decrease (or derivative of $f(x)$ is negative).
  • Show that limit of $b_n$ is zero.

Common Mistake

"Alternating signs guarantee convergence." FALSE. Alternating signs only guarantee convergence if the terms actually shrink to zero! If $b_n \to 1$, it STILL diverges by n-th term test.

Absolute vs. Conditional Convergence Preview:

If $\sum |a_n|$ converges, it converges absolutely. If $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum |a_n|$ diverges, it converges conditionally.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}$

Reasoning: Alternating harmonic. $b_n = 1/n \to 0$ and $1/(n+1) < 1/n$.

But $\sum 1/n$ diverges. Thus, it converges, but not absolutely.
Conclusion: Converges conditionally
2. $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$

Reasoning: Absolute value series $\sum 1/n^2$ converges (p=2). Absolute convergence implies convergence.

AST is valid but not needed; absolute convergence is stronger.
Conclusion: Converges absolutely
3. $\sum \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{\sqrt{n}}$

Reasoning: $b_n = 1/\sqrt{n} \to 0$ and is decreasing. AST applies.

However, absolute series $\sum 1/\sqrt{n}$ diverges (p=1/2).
Conclusion: Converges conditionally
4. $\sum (-1)^n \frac{n}{n+1}$

Reasoning: $b_n = n/(n+1) \to 1 \neq 0$.

Terms fail to go to zero. Fails n-th term test.
Conclusion: Diverges
5. $\sum (-1)^{n+1} \frac{\ln n}{n}$

Reasoning: $b_n = (\ln n)/n \to 0$ via L'Hôpital. Decreasing for $n > e$.

Absolute series diverges via Integral Test. AST gives convergence.
Conclusion: Converges conditionally
6. $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n+3}$

Reasoning: $b_n = 1/(n+3) \to 0$, decreasing. AST proves convergence.

Series without $(-1)^n$ acts like harmonic $1/n$, meaning divergence.
Conclusion: Converges conditionally
7. $\sum (-1)^n \frac{n}{n^2+1}$

Reasoning: $b_n = n/(n^2+1) \to 0$, decreasing. AST proves convergence.

Absolute series divergent (LCT with $1/n$).
Conclusion: Converges conditionally
8. $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{2^n}$

Reasoning: Alternating geometric with $r = -1/2$.

Absolute series is geometric with $r=1/2$, which converges.
Conclusion: Converges absolutely
9. $\sum (-1)^n (1+\frac{1}{n})$

Reasoning: AST requires $b_n \to 0$. Here $b_n = 1 + 1/n \to 1$.

The terms alternate between numbers close to $1$ and $-1$.
Conclusion: Diverges
10. $\sum (-1)^n \frac{1 + \sin(1/n)}{n}$

Reasoning: $b_n = \frac{1+\sin(1/n)}{n} \sim \frac{1+0}{n} = \frac{1}{n} \to 0$. By AST, converges. Absolute series acts like $1/n$ (diverges).

Conclusion: Converges conditionally

8. The Ratio Test

Standard Theorem

Let $L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right|$.

If $L < 1$, the series converges absolutely.
If $L > 1$, the series diverges.
If $L = 1$, the test is INCONCLUSIVE.

When to think of it:

When you see factorials ($n!$) or combinations of exponentials ($a^n$) and polynomials. Mandatory for determining Power Series radius of convergence.

Common Mistake

Stopping when $L=1$ and guessing. $L=1$ simply means the tests failed (like it does for all p-series). You must restart with LCT, DCT, Integral, or Alternating Series tests.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \frac{n}{2^n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)/2^{n+1}}{n/2^n} \right| = \lim \frac{n+1}{2n} = \frac{1}{2}$

$1/2 < 1$.

Conclusion: Converges
2. $\sum \frac{n!}{3^n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)!/3^{n+1}}{n!/3^n} \right| = \lim \frac{n+1}{3} = \infty$

$\infty > 1$.

Conclusion: Diverges
3. $\sum \frac{3^n}{n!}$
$\lim \left| \frac{3^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{3^n/n!} \right| = \lim \frac{3}{n+1} = 0$

$0 < 1$.

Conclusion: Converges
4. $\sum \frac{n!}{n^n}$
$\lim \frac{(n+1)!}{(n+1)^{n+1}} \frac{n^n}{n!} = \lim \frac{n^n}{(n+1)^n} = \lim \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n = \frac{1}{e}$

$1/e \approx 0.368 < 1$.

Conclusion: Converges
5. $\sum \frac{n^2}{5^n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)^2/5^{n+1}}{n^2/5^n} \right| = \lim \frac{(n+1)^2}{5n^2} = \frac{1}{5}$

$1/5 < 1$.

Conclusion: Converges
6. $\sum \frac{2^n n!}{n^n}$
$\lim \frac{2^{n+1}(n+1)!}{(n+1)^{n+1}} \frac{n^n}{2^n n!} = 2 \lim \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n = \frac{2}{e}$

$2 / 2.718 \approx 0.735 < 1$.

Conclusion: Converges
7. $\sum n!$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)!}{n!} \right| = \lim (n+1) = \infty$

$\infty > 1$.

Conclusion: Diverges
8. $\sum \frac{(n!)^2}{(2n)!}$
$\lim \frac{((n+1)!)^2}{(2n+2)!} \frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2} = \lim \frac{(n+1)^2}{(2n+2)(2n+1)} = \frac{1}{4}$

$1/4 < 1$. Note: $(2(n+1))! = (2n+2)! = (2n+2)(2n+1)(2n)!$

Conclusion: Converges
9. $\sum \frac{x^n}{n!}$
$\lim \left| \frac{x^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{x^n/n!} \right| = \lim \frac{|x|}{n+1} = 0$

$0 < 1$ for ANY $x$. This is an introduction to Power Series converging everywhere.

Conclusion: Converges for all x
10. $\sum \frac{1}{n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{1/(n+1)}{1/n} \right| = \lim \frac{n}{n+1} = 1$

CAUTION: Since $L=1$, Ratio Test tells us absolutely nothing here.

Conclusion: Inconclusive (Must look to p-series to prove divergence)

9. The Root Test

Standard Theorem

Let $L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$.

If $L < 1$, the series converges absolutely.
If $L > 1$, the series diverges.
If $L = 1$, the test is INCONCLUSIVE.

When to think of it:

When the ENTIRE sequence structure involves an n-th power, such as $(bla)^{n}$ or $(bla)^{n^2}$.

Worked Examples

1. $\sum \left(\frac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{\left(\frac{n}{2n+1}\right)^n} = \lim \frac{n}{2n+1} = \frac{1}{2}$
Conclusion: Converges ($1/2 < 1$)
2. $\sum \left(\frac{3n}{n+1}\right)^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{\dots} = \lim \frac{3n}{n+1} = 3$
Conclusion: Diverges ($3 > 1$)
3. $\sum \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{-n^2}$

Recall $\lim (1+1/n)^n = e$. We evaluate $\sum [ (1+1/n)^{-n} ]^n$.

$\lim \sqrt[n]{\dots} = \lim (1+1/n)^{-n} = e^{-1} = \frac{1}{e}$
Conclusion: Converges ($1/e \approx 0.37 < 1$)
4. $\sum \left(\frac{n}{n+2}\right)^n$

Reasoning: Taking nth root gives $\lim \frac{n}{n+2} = 1$.

Conclusion: Inconclusive (Root Test fails for $L=1$)
5. $\sum \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{| (-2/3)^n |} = \frac{2}{3}$
Conclusion: Converges absolutely ($2/3 < 1$)
6. $\sum \left(\frac{n^2+1}{3n^2+4}\right)^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{\dots} = \lim \frac{n^2+1}{3n^2+4} = \frac{1}{3}$
Conclusion: Converges ($1/3 < 1$)
7. $\sum \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right)^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{n^{-n/2}} = \lim \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = 0$
Conclusion: Converges ($0 < 1$)
8. $\sum \left(\frac{2n+5}{2n+1}\right)^n$
Root test yields $L=1$ which is inconclusive. BUT n-th term limit $\neq 0$.
Conclusion: Diverges (by n-th term test)
9. $\sum \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^{n^2}$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{\dots} = \lim \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^n = \frac{1}{e}$
Conclusion: Converges ($1/e < 1$)
10. $\sum |x|^n$
$\lim \sqrt[n]{|x|^n} = |x|$

Converges absolutely for $|x| < 1$, diverges for $|x| > 1$.

Conclusion: Leads to Power Series Radii!

Absolute vs. Conditional Convergence

When dealing with series that have both positive and negative terms, we classify their convergence into two strict types:

Absolute Convergence

A series $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely if the series of its absolute values $\sum |a_n|$ converges.

Fact: Absolute convergence guarantees convergence of the original series. (If $|a_n|$ converges, $a_n$ converges).

Example: $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges absolutely because $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges (p=2).

Conditional Convergence

A series $\sum a_n$ converges conditionally if the original series $\sum a_n$ converges, BUT the absolute value series $\sum |a_n|$ diverges.

Classic Example: The Alternating Harmonic Series $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$. It converges by AST, but $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges.

Power Series, Radius, and Interval of Convergence

A Power Series is an infinite polynomial centered at $x=c$:

$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x - c)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-c) + c_2(x-c)^2 + c_3(x-c)^3 + \dots$

How to find the Interval of Convergence:

  1. Apply the Ratio Test to the absolute value of the general term: $\lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| < 1$.
  2. Solve the resulting inequality for $|x - c| < R$. The number $R$ is the Radius of Convergence.
  3. Write the open interval $(c-R, c+R)$.
  4. MANDATORY: Test the endpoints $x = c-R$ and $x = c+R$ individually by plugging them back into the original series, removing all $x$'s, and evaluating the resulting numerical series using standard tests.

Worked Examples (Intervals of Convergence)

1. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x^n$
$\lim |x^{n+1}/x^n| = |x| < 1$

Endpoints: $x=1 \implies \sum 1^n$ (Div), $x=-1 \implies \sum (-1)^n$ (Div).

Interval: (-1, 1). $R = 1$.
2. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(x-2)^n}{n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(x-2)^{n+1}/(n+1)}{(x-2)^n/n} \right| = |x-2| < 1$

Endpoints $x=1, x=3$.
$x=3 \implies \sum 1/n$ (Div p-series).
$x=1 \implies \sum (-1)^n/n$ (Conv AST).

Interval: [1, 3). $R = 1$.
3. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n(x+1)^n}{3^n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)(x+1)^{n+1}/3^{n+1}}{n(x+1)^n/3^n} \right| = \frac{|x+1|}{3} < 1 \implies |x+1| < 3$

Endpoints $x=-4, 2$. Both yield $n(\pm 1)^n$, failing n-th term test.

Interval: (-4, 2). $R = 3$.
4. $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(x-4)^n}{n!}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(x-4)^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{(x-4)^n/n!} \right| = \lim \frac{|x-4|}{n+1} = 0 < 1$

Limit is 0 for ANY $x$. Converges everywhere.

Interval: $(-\infty, \infty)$. $R = \infty$.
5. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n! x^n}{5^n}$
$\lim \left| \frac{(n+1)! x^{n+1}/5^{n+1}}{n! x^n/5^n} \right| = \lim \frac{(n+1)|x|}{5} = \infty$

Only converges when $x=0$.

Interval: {0}. $R = 0$.
6. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(x-1)^n}{n^2}$
$\lim |(x-1)| < 1$. Endpoints $0, 2$.

$x=2 \implies \sum 1/n^2$ (Conv).
$x=0 \implies \sum (-1)^n/n^2$ (Conv).

Interval: [0, 2]. $R = 1$.
7. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(x+3)^n}{n 2^n}$
$\lim \frac{|x+3|}{2} < 1 \implies |x+3| < 2$. Endpoints $-5, -1$.

$x=-1 \implies \sum 1/n$ (Div).
$x=-5 \implies \sum (-1)^n/n$ (Conv AST).

Interval: [-5, -1). $R = 2$.
8. $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n(x-5)^n$
$\lim |x-5| < 1$. Endpoints 4, 6.

$x=6 \implies \sum n$ (Div).
$x=4 \implies \sum n(-1)^n$ (Div).

Interval: (4, 6). $R = 1$.

Taylor & Maclaurin Series

A Taylor Series is a power series that exactly matches a function $f(x)$ at $x=c$ and all its derivatives:

$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(c)}{n!} (x - c)^n = f(c) + f'(c)(x-c) + \frac{f''(c)}{2!}(x-c)^2 + \frac{f'''(c)}{3!}(x-c)^3 + \dots$

A Maclaurin Series is just a Taylor series centered at $c=0$.

Common Standard Series (Must Memorize)

Function Maclaurin Series Interval
$e^x$ $\sum \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \dots$ $(-\infty, \infty)$
$\sin x$ $\sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \frac{x^5}{5!} - \dots$ $(-\infty, \infty)$
$\cos x$ $\sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^4}{4!} - \dots$ $(-\infty, \infty)$
$\frac{1}{1-x}$ $\sum x^n = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \dots$ $(-1, 1)$
$\ln(1+x)$ $\sum \frac{(-1)^{n-1} x^n}{n} = x - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} - \dots$ $(-1, 1]$

Building New Series

Instead of computing derivatives, substitute into known Maclaurin series:

1. Find Maclaurin for $f(x) = x^2 e^{3x}$
Sub $3x$ into $e^x$: $e^{3x} = \sum \frac{(3x)^n}{n!}$
Multiply by $x^2$: $\sum \frac{3^n x^{n+2}}{n!}$
2. Find Maclaurin for $f(x) = \int \sin(x^2) dx$
Sub $x^2$ into $\sin x$: $\sum \frac{(-1)^n (x^2)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+2}}{(2n+1)!}$
Integrate term-by-term: $C + \sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+3}}{(4n+3)(2n+1)!}$

Error Bounds

Alternating Series Error

If an alternating series satisfies the AST, the error by stopping at $n$ terms is bounded by the magnitude of the very next term.

$|S - S_n| \le |a_{n+1}|$
Example:

How many terms of $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ are needed to be within 0.01 of the true sum?

We need $|a_{n+1}| \le 0.01 \implies \frac{1}{(n+1)^2} \le 0.01 \implies (n+1)^2 \ge 100 \implies n+1 \ge 10 \implies n \ge 9$.

Lagrange Error Bound

For ANY Taylor polynomial approximation, the error remainder $R_n(x)$ is bounded by:

$|R_n(x)| \le \frac{M}{(n+1)!} |x - c|^{n+1}$

Where $M$ is the MAX value of $|f^{(n+1)}(z)|$ between $c$ and $x$.

Example:

Bound error of $P_2(x)$ for $\cos x$ at $x=0.1$.

$n=2$, $c=0$, $x=0.1$. Next derivative $f'''(z) = \sin(z)$. Max of $|\sin z|$ is $\le 1$. $M=1$.
$|R_2| \le \frac{1}{3!} |0.1 - 0|^3 = \frac{0.001}{6}$.

Test Selection Strategy (The Flowchart)

Stuck on a series? Try this mental flowchart in exactly this order:

  1. 1 n-th Term Test

    Just look at the terms as $n \to \infty$. Do they fail to go to zero? If so, DIVERGES. YOU ARE DONE.

  2. 2 Is it a special series?

    Look for $ar^n$ (Geometric, check $|r|<1$) or $1/n^p$ (p-Series, check $p>1$).

  3. 3 Does it have $(-1)^n$?

    Use Alternating Series Test. Verify terms shrink to 0. Follow up with absolute vs conditional check.

  4. 4 Does it resemble a p-series, but is messy?

    Use Limit Comparison Test. Take the highest degree $n$ terms from numerator/denominator.

  5. 5 Does it have factorials or $c^n$?

    Use Ratio Test. This is the king of factorials.

  6. 6 Is it something easily integrable?

    Use Integral Test. E.g., $1/(x \ln x)$. Check positive, continuous, decreasing.

AP Exam Survival Traps to Avoid

  • Ratio Test yielding $L=1$ does not mean it diverges. It means you must try another test.
  • Always check endpoints for Interval of Convergence. They are worth 1-2 points on the FRQ!
  • Remember the $+ C$ when integrating to form a new series, and solve for $C$ by plugging in the center point.
