What Your Report Should Look Like

One PDF. No loose files. A professional report flows like this:

  • Introduction: One short paragraph — what data are you analyzing and why.
  • Methods & Results: One section per task (six total). State the method you chose and justify it, show your R code and output, check assumptions, and write a plain-English conclusion. Imagine explaining it to a friend who took Bio 101, not a statistician.
  • Discussion & Conclusion: Step back and summarize. What did you find? What are the limitations? What would you tell me — the person whose data this is — about my activity habits?
Write for a real audience. Your final interpretation for every task should be something I could read without knowing what a p-value is and still understand what you found. If your conclusion says "we reject H₀ at α = 0.05" and nothing else, that's incomplete.

The Questions

  1. Am I actually an "active" person by the numbers?

    Popular fitness apps and health guidelines often cite 8,500 steps per day as the benchmark for an "active" lifestyle. Using the method assigned to your last-name band, evaluate whether my average daily step count is consistent with this benchmark. Report a 95% CI for the true mean and interpret it in context — do the data support the claim that I meet this threshold, or fall short?

  2. Do I walk more on weekdays or weekends?

    At first glance, you might expect weekends to have higher step counts — more free time, maybe a long walk or errands. But teaching days also mean a lot of movement between classrooms and offices. Using the method assigned to your band, evaluate whether a statistically significant difference in average steps exists between weekdays and weekends. Provide a 95% CI for the difference and explain what that interval tells you in plain English.

  3. A bold claim about hitting the step goal.

    Someone claims I hit the 10,000-step goal on more than 90% of days during this period. Before attempting any analysis, carefully check whether all required mathematical conditions for this type of claim are satisfied. Show your work. If any condition fails, state clearly which one and why — and explain what that means for your ability to draw conclusions from this data.

  4. Is hitting the step goal related to the day of the week?

    It seems intuitive that weekdays (long campus days) might produce different step-goal outcomes than weekends (rest days). But is that actually supported by the data? Determine whether a statistically significant relationship exists between DayType and MeetGoal. Report all relevant statistics — including a two-way frequency summary with both observed and expected values — and interpret your findings. Address any conditions that may affect the reliability of your conclusion.

  5. Did my activity level change across the three phases of the semester?

    The dataset is split into three 15-day phases: Phase1 (Jan 20–Feb 3), Phase2 (Feb 4–Feb 18), and Phase3 (Feb 19–Mar 5). Determine whether the average step count is the same across all three phases or whether at least one phase stands out. Report the complete test output, a 95% CI for each group mean, and include side-by-side boxplots. If results are significant, discuss which phases appear to differ and what that might mean.

  6. Can step count help predict active minutes?

    The Pixel Watch 3 logs steps and active minutes independently — but they should be related. Determine whether Steps can serve as a useful predictor of ActiveMinutes. Report the fitted equation, the proportion of variability explained, a 95% CI for the rate of change, and a scatterplot with the fitted line overlaid. Interpret the rate of change in plain English: if I walk 1,000 more steps tomorrow, how many more active minutes should I expect the watch to record?
