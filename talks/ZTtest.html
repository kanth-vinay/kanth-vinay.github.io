Scenario 1: Test of Average Commute Time

A city planner is interested in determining whether the average commute time in the city has increased from the long-established average of 30 minutes. She collects a random sample of 35 commute times from residents across various neighborhoods. In previous studies conducted by the Department of Transportation, extensive data on commute times were collected over many years, providing a wealth of information for analysis. The planner believes this data may help in her analysis of the new average.

Answer and Explanation:

This scenario uses a z-test since there is an indication that the population standard deviation is known (implied by previous studies and existing data). The sample size is also large (n > 30), which further supports the use of a z-test.

Scenario 2: Average Daily Caloric Intake

A nutritionist wants to investigate whether the average daily caloric intake of adults in a rural region has shifted from the recommended 2,000 calories. She randomly selects 15 individuals and records their caloric intake. She notices that this population tends to have irregular meal patterns, and she does not have any previous detailed records about caloric intake from this region. However, the intake data she gathers appears somewhat skewed.

Answer and Explanation:

This scenario calls for the Wilcoxon’s test. The sample size is small (n < 30), the data is skewed, and there’s no prior information about the population distribution or standard deviation, indicating that a non-parametric test like Wilcoxon’s would be suitable.

Scenario 3: Mean Temperature in a Region

A meteorologist suspects that the winter temperatures in a specific mountainous region have dropped below the historical average of 32°F due to climate change. Over a period of two months, she records a random sample of 50 daily temperatures. The region has been extensively studied by climate scientists for decades, and a large dataset exists on temperature patterns. She uses this data to compare current temperatures with historical values.

Answer and Explanation:

This scenario calls for a z-test since there is a large sample size (n > 30) and the presence of historical data implies that the population standard deviation is known.

Scenario 4: Exam Performance in a Class

A teacher is curious whether her class of 8 students performed better than the national average on a standardized math exam, where the national average score is reported to be 70. The teacher observes some variability in her students’ performance and notes that they do not seem to follow a strict pattern in their scores. However, the scores come from a high-achieving class that tends to skew higher than average.

Answer and Explanation:

This situation suggests the use of a t-test, as the sample size is small (n < 30), and there is no indication that the population standard deviation is known.

Scenario 5: Test of Average Blood Pressure

A doctor is concerned that the average systolic blood pressure of her patients may be higher than the healthy benchmark of 120 mmHg. She collects data from a sample of 25 patients who are all on the same treatment plan. While her hospital has been collecting data on blood pressure for years, the distribution of blood pressure readings in her small group of patients seems to vary more than expected.

Answer and Explanation:

A t-test is appropriate here. The sample size is small (n < 30), and there’s variability in the data, with no clear indication that the population standard deviation is known.

Scenario 6: Average Salary of Employees

A large company is reviewing its employee salaries to determine if they are paying above the industry standard of $50,000 per year. A random sample of 40 employees is chosen from different departments across the company. The company has access to historical salary data from its HR department, as it has been tracking salaries for many years and knows how salaries fluctuate over time.

Answer and Explanation:

This calls for a z-test. The sample size is large (n > 30), and the company has historical salary data, implying that the population standard deviation is likely known.

Scenario 7: Testing Sleep Hours

A university health researcher wants to determine whether students at the college are getting less than the recommended 8 hours of sleep each night. She tracks the sleeping patterns of 12 students over two weeks and notices considerable variation in their sleeping habits. Additionally, she has no prior studies or baseline data on the sleeping patterns of students at this university.

Answer and Explanation:

A t-test would be suitable here. The sample size is small (n < 30), and there’s no indication that the population standard deviation is known, as this is the researcher’s first investigation on this topic.

Scenario 8: Test of Fuel Efficiency

A car manufacturer claims that their new model achieves a fuel efficiency of 25 miles per gallon (mpg) or better. To test this, 10 cars are randomly selected, and their fuel efficiency is measured. The manufacturer has previously conducted fuel efficiency tests on earlier models, but the new model’s performance may differ from historical data.

Answer and Explanation:

A t-test is appropriate here. The sample size is small (n < 30), and although there’s historical data, it might not fully apply to the new model’s performance, so we can’t assume the population standard deviation is known.

Scenario 9: Test of a Medication's Effect

A pharmaceutical company is interested in determining whether a new medication lowers blood pressure by more than the current standard treatment. They collect a sample of 20 patients who are administered the new medication. Previous studies on other medications have been done, but the new medication is expected to behave differently.

Answer and Explanation:

A t-test is appropriate here since the sample size is relatively small (n < 30) and the medication is new, so the population standard deviation is not known.

Scenario 10: Test of Machine Output

A factory uses a machine that produces screws with an average diameter of 5 mm. The manager suspects that the machine's output has changed and conducts a random sample of 45 screws to test the average diameter. The factory has maintained detailed records of screw production for years, providing valuable data on the machine’s historical performance.

Answer and Explanation:

A z-test would be suitable here since the sample size is large (n > 30) and the factory has historical data, implying that the population standard deviation is likely known.
